POETRY

What is Poetry?

Poetry is an imaginative rendition that gives powerful experience to expressions, idea; feelings are in a metaphoric/figurative language. It is a piece of literary work that expresses deep feelings, thoughts and human experiences and may use imagery, figurative language, sound devices and rhythm to create an effect. The language and style in poetry is usually derated and noble. Poetry is normally arranged in lines and groups known as stanzas. The beauty of poetry is more appreciated when it is read ahead to the hearing of the audience.

Types of Poetry

1.     Ballad: It is a poem, song transmitted orally which tells a story. Ballads are communicated orally, among illiterate or only partly literature people. The themes are based on popular stories of legends and great village heroes. It also narrates the story of unfulfilled lone, death of a loved one e.t.c.

2.     Lyric: A lyric is a fairly short poem consisting of the utterance by a single speaker who expresses a state of mind or a process of perception, thought and feeling. It is a poem that is accompanied by the use of a musical instrument. It is a poem that expresses strong personal emotions like line which is usually light hearted.

3.     Ode: It is a long lyric poem that is serious in subject and treatment and has elaborate style. It is spoken in the form of an address to somebody or something. It is written to praise and glorify somebody.

4.     Elegy/Dirge: It is a song on anexpression of grief on the occasion of a particular person's death. It is always characterised by sorrowful or lamentable tone of mood. When it comes in a written form, it is called Elegy.

5.     Epic: It is a long verse narrative poem told in a formal and elevated style and centred on great deeds. It is written in grand style for it deals with the exploits. Example is John Miltons "Paradise lost".

6.     Idyll: t is a short description poem of rural or pastoral life. It is a short, smooth and idealized poem that describes a peaceful and happy scene or incident.

7.     Sonnet: It is a lyric poem of fourteen iambic pentameter lines. Two types of sonnet are often identified.

·     English or Shakespearean sonnet and Italian or Petrarchan sonnet.

a.     Shakespearean sonnet consists of three quatrains and a final rhyming complete. The rhyming scheme of Shakespearean sonnet is ABAB CDCD EFEF GG

b.     The Petrarchan sonnet is divided into two parts. The first part consists of eight lines [Octave] with a rhyming scheme ABBA ABBA while the second part consists of six lines [sestet] with a rhyming scheme CDC CDC, CDD CDD or CDE CDE.

8.     Pastoral poetry: It is a long and episode poems concerning rural/pastoral life.

9.     Didactic poetry: A didactic poem teaches moral

10.     Narrative poetry: A narrative poem tells a story.

11.     Lullaby: A soft song meant to keep children quiet, peaceful, sleep and happy.

12.     Limerick: A kind of nonsense but humorous five- line structured short poem.

Literary Elements in Poetry

1.     Rhythm: The regularity of the intervals between one syllable and another or the changes in the voice at intervals. It creates a sort of regular flow of pattern on a poem.

2.     Rhyme: It is the sameness in sound or words ending usually at the end of the poetry. Rhyme could be a double rhyme, half rhyme in internal rhyme.

When it is internal rhyme the words or sounds correspond with the syllable at the end of each line.

e.g. Twinkle twinkle little star          A

How I wonder what you are           A

Up above the world so high          A

Like a diamond in the sky          A

Double rhyme: This compound with two final syllable.

e.g. Ladies and gent, you are here assembled           A

To hear why earth and heaven tremble          A

Because of the black and sinister arts               B

Of an Irish writer in foreign part               B

Half rhyme: These is correspondence of sound which is only partly complete

e.g. There was a whispering in my heart          A

A sign of the coal                         B

Grown wistful of a former earth               A

It might be recall                         B

3.     Symbolism: The usage of objects or images to represent abstract ideas.

4.     Stanza: It is a division in the formal pattern of poem. It also refers to a group of lines forming a complete division of a poem. The arrangement of stanza are as follows:

i.     Two lines stanza Couplet

ii.     Three lines stanza Triplet

iii.     Four lines stanza Quatrain

iv.     Five lines stanza Quintet

v.     Six lines stanza Sestet

vi.     Seven lines stanza Septet

vii.     Eight lines stanza Octave

5.     Rhyme scheme: The pattern of sounds which end the lines of a poem based on the sameness or differences between the sounds.

6.     Rhyme royal: A stanza form of seven decasyllabic lines rhyming ababbc.

7.     Blame Verse: A blame verse consists of lines of iambic pentameter without end rhyme.

8.     Free Verse: A free verse occurs when the lines do not have a regular metre and do not count in rhyme.

9.     Foot: A unit of metre in a line of poetry.

Types

Minismetre 1foot

Dimetre 2 feet

Trimetre 3 feet

Tetrametre 4 feet

Pentrametre 5 feet

Hexametre 6 feet

Octametre 8 feet

10.     Metre: The recurrence of a particular rhythmic flow in poetry.

11.     Cacophony: The difficulty in the flour of sounds in poetry which is usually created by strange words.

12.     Cadence: The rising and falling of the rhythm of speech.

13.     Caesura: A pause or break on a line of poetry.

14.     Canto: A sub division of a long narrative poem of an epic poetry.

15.     Poetic license: The imaginative and linguistic freedom which allows poets to depart from the normal prose standard discourse.

PIANO AND DRUMS BY GABRIEL OKARA


When at break of day a riverside

I hear the jungle drums telegraphing

The mystic rhythm, urgent, raw

Like bleeding flesh, speaking of

Primal youth and the beginning

I see the panther ready to pounce

The leopard snarling about to leap

And he hunters crouch with spears poised


And my blood ripples, turns torrents,

Topples the years and at once I'm

In my mother's laps a suckling

At once I'm walking simple

Paths with no innovations

Rugged, fashioned with the naked

Warmth of hurrying feet and groping hearts

In green leaves and wild flowers pulsing


Then I hear a wailing piano

Solo speaking of complex ways

In tear-turnover concrete;

Of faraway lands

And new horizons with

Coaxing diminuendo, counterpoint,

Crescendo. But lost in the labyrinth

Of its complexities, it ends in the middle

Of a phrase at a daggerpoint


And I lost in the morning mist

Of an age at a riverside keep

Wondering in the mystic rhythm

Of jungle drums and the concerto


ANALYSIS

The poem typically describes the post-colonial African societies where there is a contrast between the traditional African culture and the Western Culture, a fact illustrated in the first three stanzas of the poem. The first and second stanzas illustrate a typical African setting while the third described the Western Culture which makes the clash of both cultures leave the personal ultimately perplexed in the fourth and last stanza.

Poem opens with persona being at a riverside (line 1) where he hears jungle drums that represents the African culture which is characterized by primal and simple happenings as explained with the words jungle (line 2), primal / beginning (line 5), hunters (line 8), simple / paths (line 12/13) and rugged (line 14).

The drums talk about the African society before colonialism; the primordial times (line 5). During this period, man is in sync with nature, as there is more interaction of Africans with the wild. Also, premedical professions like hunting is predominant as hunters are seen with their weapons ready for hunt (line 8).

The description continues in stanza how where the persona reacts to all these memories that flash before his eyes and he is moved (line 9) by the cajoling and persistent, beats of the drum in the first stanza. He remembers his childhood (line 11) and the years be spend living the simple lite of an African, shared-by others, a life filled with communal living, emotions and warmth (Lines 12-16).

Suddenly there is an interruption in the euphonic reminisce of the persona as he hears another instrument different from the drums, a piano (line 17). The piano, which is the Western Culture, is difficult and foreign to the persona (line 18) but it cannot be ignored as it is being introduced to him already, though not in a palatable way. The piano is described as wailing (line 17) which shows that it is not acceptable neither is it enjoyable. The newly introduced culture is complex as the persona uses elevated diction to describe its intricacies from line 18 through 25. The culture is complex, foreign, compelling and uncomfortable but this is not only experienced by the persona. As suddenly as the culture is introduced, it is withdrawn after brewing confusion in the persona (line 23 - 25).

The last stanza eventually explains the dilemma of the persona, an African who is a product of post-colonialism. He is caught between the pull of two cultures which are different in all aspects. His love for the simple African life is deep but the Western culture which is introduced has interacted with the primal one-a process which makes neither culture whole. This, the persona like every other African is torn between these two cultures which are different in all aspects. His love for the simple African life is deep but the Western culture which life is introduced has interacted with the primal one - a process which makes neither cultural whole. Thus, the persona like every other African is torn between these two cultures and there is no way for separation. He has to live with the new culture (line 28) that these two ways of live has formed and is forced to dance to the tune of both the primal and the Western Cultures (line 29).


THEMES

1.     INTERACTION OF CULTURES: This also known as dash or contract of cultures. In the poem, there is a time where there is dominance of the African culture in stanza one and two before the introduction of the foreign Western Culture which dominates stanza three. Then, in stanza four the new culture produced by both cultures acting upon each other is explained; a new phenomenon not much appreciated and greatly confusing.


2.     CULTURE: The two cultures, African and Western, are explained in the poem in ways which best capture what they are in reality. The African culture is simple and the persona loves and misses it. Thus, the poet uses simple diction to explain and describe it. Also Africa culture is primal, rugged and closer to nature, making it somewhat dangerous. This is explained with the use of the words - primal, partner, leopard, hunters, rugged, leaves, and flowers.


On the other hand, Western culture is considered complex, foreign and confusing. In line 17, the Western culture is introduced, showing that it used to be absent - emphasizing its foreign nature to the persona and other Africans (line 20). Moreover, the sound emitted by the piano is complex and this is explained using elevated language such as concerts, diminuendo, counterpoint, crescendo and complexities. "Labyrinth" used in line 23 further strengthens the reality that western culture is complicated and confusing like a maze. Irrespective of the fact that it is withdrawn from Africa suddenly, as soon as colonialism ends, the remnants of its complexities still remain a leaving the persona confused.


3.     CONFUSION: This theme dominates the last stanza of the poem. Here, the persona used to have one way of life that he loved and cherished before another culture was thrust upon him. In the end, the cultures interact and this leaves him distraught and confused in the world created by this interaction. His new world is considered 'mystic' (line 28) - the best word to describe a mysterious and strange situation. He then wanders like someone trapped in a maze. There is no going back to the initial way of life as an irreversible damage has been done to these that dwell in this 'age' (line 29) of post-colonialism.


4.     NATURE: The poem also talks about the natural world which is characteristics of the African culture. This world consists of all things predating human technology, production and design, a world before colonialism. Lines 6 through 8 describe a typical forest where animals are ready to charge at prey or threats and hunters posed to hunt their prey too, hunting being one of the dominant professions in the natural world. Lines 12 and 13 further explains a world without technology, science or innovations, instead, crude means are used in getting things done. Lastly, like 16 describe the natural scenery filled with plants and flowers, obviously grown through natural means.

POETIC DEVICES

1.     IMAGERY: Imagery is an element of poetry which creates a mental image in the readers or listeners of a poetic work. In this poem, as the reader reads through, she visualize a lot of scenery, feels and hears as though she were the persona in the poem. Visual imagery which appeals to the sense of sight is used in lines 6 through 8 where the reader visualizes a forest and line 12 through 15 where the mental image of foot paths in a typical African village is formed. Auditory imagery is also used in line 2/3, and stanza three to illustrate hearing the sounds of the drums and piano. Lastly, kinesthetic imagery dominates stanza two and four where actions and movements are represented with words. The use of this imagery allows the reader to live the poem as the words come alive, enabling her see, feel, hear and move with all that transpires in the poem


2.     SIMILE: This is the companion of two entities with the use of 'like' or 'as'. Simile is used in lines ¾ - '…..raw/like bleeding flesh'.

3.     PERSONIFICATION: This is the attribution of human qualities to inanimate objects. In line 16, flower 'pulsed' and a piano 'wailed' in line 17 are both human qualities.


4.     SYNECDOCHE: It is the representation of a whole with its part or vice versa. "Feet" and 'heart' in line 15 are parts of the human body, thus they are used to represent humans.


5.     ALLITERATION: This is figure of sound where there is a repetition of initial consonant sounds in words they are in close proximity in a line of poetry. In line 6 'p' alliterates in 'panther' and 'pounce' while I alliterate in 'leopard' and 'leap' in line 7.

ANALYSIS

The poem is based on the cycle of life concentrating mainly on the negative aspects and the various hindrances, posing as inhibitions to attaining one's goal. Stanza one explains that as one grows, he gets conscious of the fact that old age draws nearer as each day passes. The journey of one's life to adulthood isn't a rushed one. Rather, it is a slow and steady voyage through life.

At twenty, as stated in stanza two, man's hope is great and he has high expectations for his life. At this stage of life, there is opportunity to dream because there is enough time to achieve these dreams. There is also will and lots of opportunities, as well as strength to explore the world in order to realize this dreams.

Stanza three in two sentences records the beginning of man's dilemma once he reaches thirty years. Now, the body starts to feel the effects of the strength exerted though still in the youthful phase. These pain are considered normal because laboratory tests for illness show nothing. The pains are just the normal effects of being alive and using the body.


Pain, being an inhibition and indication of wear and tear of the body, is the beginning stanza four explains how an adult's life is riddled with responsibilities. As an adult, there is need to attend to the domestic needs that are present in having a family. As a result, there can be no sudden leaps at the moon now (lines 15/16), meaning he has to live his life according to plan; no more spontaneity. On the other hand, this can also mean a furtherance of pain which cannot allow for such exerting movement anymore.

Stanza five then brings forth the result of all the dreams, aspirations, labour and work that a man has done in his youth. Sadly, the results is 'red ink' (line 18) which symbolizes low grades or little to no success. At adulthood life is filled with failures and disappointment with no accomplishment to boast of the only accomplishment with no accomplishment to boast of the only accomplishment in stanza 6 are children who are not even regarded as achievement because child bearing is considered a natural thing in line 24 (no specialist effort there).

In stanza seven, reference is made to the Biblical verse which states that man will live for 70years but the poem doesn't even hold that to heart as it isn't sure. In the end in stanza eight, when man becomes old, he reflects on the life he has lived and is filled with regrets and dissatisfaction. However, he can do no more at this stage as he then succumb to what the world dictates to him in form of the money he has his children and the help he gets. He can hardly make choices for himself or steer the course anymore.


THEMES

1.     MORTALITY: The poem emphasizes the fact that everyone who is born will eventually grow old and die. This stage of the human existence cannot be stopped until one gets to the final destination which is death, after old age.


2.     OLD AGE: Old age is the main theme of the poem. A person's life has more meaning to himself at old age. At this period, one counts one's blessing and laments all unattained goals. He is no longer comfortable in his body which now riddles with pains at every joint. Once a person gets to the old age, he can't always go wherever he desires and neither can be dream of acquiring the world. All he has left to do is succumb to the world in resigned fate and accept all that are the result of what he does in his youth.


3.     ANXIETY: In the poem, old age is not exactly welcomed with open arms. It is feared and as a person creeps steadily towards old age, he fears what is will bring for him. He panics, as the title of the poem states, at how his life moves right into old age, from the much desired youthfulness.


4.     CYCLE OF LIFE: Line after line, stanza after another, the poem describe human life from childhood through adulthood. At these different stages of the cycle of life, there are things present and those that need to be achieved. At twenty, man dreams of building the whole world and acquiring it for himself as he has strength and time by his side but as he grows older as early as thirty years, his strength dwindles and is faced with factures, regrets and un-accomplishments which come to stay with him all through old age.


5.     INHIBITIONS: The poem also lists a lot of things which hinder a person in realizing his goals. In stanza three, it is pain while in stanza four, the inhibition is domestic affairs in the home. And lastly, in stanza eight, which is the last stanza, old age itself doesn't allow a person achieve anymore of his dreams which he falls to fulfill at his youthful age.

POETIC DEVICES

1.     ALLUSION: This is a reference to a historical period, religious belief, popular occurrence or another text in a literacy work. Lines 25 through 27 of the poem makes reference to Psalm 90 verse 10 in the Bible, where it is stated that man will live for 70 or 80 years. This is known as Biblical allusion.


2.     RECEPTION: This is the use of a word or words form or words for more than once in a poem. This is used to create emphasis on the words and the meaning they have. In lines 6 and 25 & 27 'hope' is repeated while 'year' is repeated in line 4


3.     METAPHOR: This is the indirect comparison of two or more entities in a literacy work. In the first stanza, growing old is likened to a bird with wings.


4.     IMAGERY: This is mental image created by a poem in the mind of its renders or listeners or listeners. The poem contains a lot of kinesthetic imagery as well as visual.

ANALYSIS

Ambush, as its name implies is a poem which narrates the way the leaders of African nations conceal themselves and lie in wait to attack unsuspecting citizens by surprise. These leaders leave no stone unturned in their guest to make life miserable for the masses. They cause poverty, hunger, disaster, strife and are ever ready to wage war against those who wish to change the status quo.

The land is used to represent the political leaders who are likened to different kinds of dangerous animals. In stanza one, political leaders are like the giant whale which surfs the sea. This whale swallows all the fishing equipment that are thrown into the sea, and since the fisherman cannot dive into the large water to catch fish with his hands, he laments his loss and wasted effort and can do nothing but return home empty-handed. This situation explains how the common people toil hard day and night, trying to make ends meet, but their hard work will always go down the drain because their leaders will make everything drown in the sea of recession, like in prices, poverty, monopoly and corruption. Instead of meeting with Jesus, like the biblical Peter and catching a lot of fish, they meet with the giant whale which makes sure they leave more wretched than they have come.

In stanza two, the land is no longer a giant whale but a saber toothed tiger, which is now extinct and very dangerous, with its long eeriness extending to rest on its lips. The sound of this tiger scares the young ones and old alike and they all cease to go about making any more moves to make things better. Thus, the youth with agile body, clear and developed mind are scared away from the helm of affairs while the state elders also choose to fold their arms, lest they get burned.

Stanza three records that the land is now a giant hawk that roams the sky, courting disaster as it moves. How, the torments of the African nations are in the water, on the land and high up in the sky, they everywhere. They bring disaster, hunger, poverty and suffering with them as they continue in their evil ways and there is no hope of them desisting. In the last stanza, this evil does not desist as the land continues to lie in ambush awaiting those who will rise up in the nearest future to try to change things for the better. At that point, they too will be snatched up like those who have tried before them and thrown into misery.

THEMES

1.     BAD LEADERSHIP: This theme is present all through the poem as it explains how much bad leadership has affected the growth of the African nations. These leaders have nothing good to offer, yet they make a living at the expense of the people and prevent those who are ready to rule justly from getting to the position of power.


2.     MISERY: As a result of the bad leadership and corrupt practices plaguing this nation, the people are thrown into hopelessness, misery, loss and disaster. They bear the brunt of disaster, losses, recession and hike in prices.


3.     REVOLUTION: In the last stanza, there is a little bit of hope that maybe one day, there will be a change in the status quo and some political leaders who are true and just will hold positions of power. However, the poem is quick to note that the bad leaders will continue to sit in hiding, waiting to devour these revolutionaries.


POETIC DEVICES

1.     ALLUSION: In the poem, in line 6, a reference is made to the tale of Peter in the Bible, who is at sea but toils to no avail to catch a fish. He meets Jesus and he is able to catch of lot of fish. In the poem, it is stated that the opposite is the case as the fisherman here go home not only with no fish but also lose their fishing equipment to the sea.


2.     RUN: This is play on words. In line 7, there is a play on the word 'peter' to make; peters with petered out desires.


3.     METAPHOR: This is a comparison without the use of 'as' or 'like'. In the first lines of stanza one to three, the land is directly compared to a giant whale, a saber-toothed tiger and a giant hawk. This is known as a metaphor.


4.     REPETITION: In the poem, 'the land' is constantly repeated. This is to make emphasis and draw attention to the fact that it is just open person (the bad leaders) that keep wreaking this great havoc on the people, without remorse.

The poem is about a war that happens in Sierra Leone decades back. The poem narrates how the war affect the Sierra Leoneans including children and on lookers. The effects of this war is unpalatable as it brings pain, hunger and sickness to all.

He likes the reason for the war which is change or probably freedom but he is not a lover of fire, symbolizing violence, death and wounds which come with it.

In sum, the effects of war are so great that it is not worth it, no matter what is hoped to be achieved or how good the intension for starting the war in the first place.

ANALYSIS

The poem talks about the aftermath of ignoring the heritage bestowed upon Africans by our ancestors. In the present day Africa, there is s high level of disregard for the African culture, tradition and customs which have been passed down to us by those that live before us, especially those that existed before colonization. As a result, there is a lot of suffering and hardship on the people us. This suffering, however, will not cease because we have ignored our heritage and our ancestors will not listen to your clamoring because of this.

Stanza one is condescending as it predicts that certainly, we 'shall one day have to tell (line 2) of our sufferings and complain out loud like beggars (line 4) but no one will listen to us'. The stanza is written in a condescending tone that mocks all that complains and as no one will listen.

Stanza two further echoes the complaints and cry for help, albeit now loudly, as the suffering increases (line 6) but still no one will listen to our pain. This continues through stanza three, still with no one ready to listen not to talk of alleviating the suffering.

One will wonder why there is no much clamoring for relief yet no help is forthcoming. Stanza four starts to make things clearer. It explains why this suffering will definitely continue. When 'our dead' i.e. the present generation, meets with 'their' dead (our ancestors), they will still not listen to us because when they spoke to us, we do not listen but instead disregarded them.

Many wonder how the dead can communicate to the living and stanza five explains that they do not rise up and talk to us directly but they have passed across their message across on the water, in the air and everywhere around us. However, we have chosen to ignore, making us unworthy sons.

The last stanza has finally in its tone, stating that since we have chosen to ignore the dead and not care for what they stand for when they are alive, then help will definitely not be forthcoming to alleviate the suffering we now face in form of the many havoc that now face the African nations, like poverty, hunger, bad leadership, war, strife and corruption.


POETIC DEVICES

1.     RHETORICAL QUESTION: This is a literacy device where a question is asked but requires no answer. Instead, it is used to drive home a point as its answer is already embedded in the question itself or in the context in which it is placed. Rhetorical questions are present in lines 3, 5, 8, 10, 11, 14, 29 and 30.


2.     REPETITION: In the poem, there is a lot of repetition of some lines, to create emphasis on the point they make. This is found in lines 3 and 5 where 'without laughter' is repeated; 'what eyes will watch our large mouths?' in lines 8 and 10; and 'what hearts will listen to your clamoring' in lines 11 and 29.


3.     SIMILE: This is a direct comparison of two entities with the use of 'like' or 'as'. It is used in line 13 'which grows in us like a tumor?'


4.     PERSONIFICATION: This is the attribution of human quality to inanimate objects, animals or abstract notions. Example is 'when our Dead comes with their Dead, when they have spoken to us in their clumsy voices', (line 15/46). Normally, the dead cannot talk.


5.     SYNECDOCHE: It is a figure of speech where a part is used to represent a whole or a whole used to represent a part. In lines 11 and 29, (what hearts will listen to our clamoring), 'hearts' which is an organ of the human being is used to represent human beings.


6.     SARCASM: This figure of speech is used to mock. The poem is full of condescending and mocking words like 'voices' of beggars' (line 4); 'large mouths' (line 8) and plaintive throats (line 14).

THEMES

1.     EFFECT OF COLONIALISM: The poem castigates the character of the post-colonial Africans. Before colonialism, the African heritage had a strong root amongst those that are now dead, and they used to cherish it. After colonialism, however a new culture is introduced and the present day Africans welcome it and embrace it, at the expense of their own culture and tradition. This act of abandonment has in turn bred nothing but suffering, violence, poverty war, strife and bad leadership.


2.     AFRICAN HERITAGE: The poem laments the neglect of the African heritage which is rich. Signs and remnants of this culture passed on from generation to generation is still very much alive around us in the water, air, mountains and within us, though we have chosen to ignore it. Until we do so though, our suffering is sure to continue.


3.     DEATH: In the poem, references are made to those who used to live but are now dead. Death being certain, they have passed on the rich culture they bear before they move on to the great beyond. In line 15, it is evident that these that are alive are still going to die and when these dead meet with the ancestors, they too will not listen, just as we ignored their ways.

The poem by Kofi Awoonor, being an African poem, addresses the issue of post-colonialism. The poem does not abhor colonialism though, instead it envisages a form of coalition between the African culture and the Western culture. Then, a new culture formed with remnants of the past and matches from the Western culture, will be the new norm.

Stanza one starts with the African culture placed between 'the anvil and the hammer' to bring forth a new culture. The anvil and hammer are instruments used by a blacksmith in beating metals into shape. The hot metal is placed on the anvil and the hammer is used to beat the metal into the desired form. Thus, once a metal goes between the anvil and the hammer, it comes out better and transformed. Lines 2-4 further describe the newness and desirability of the new culture that will be birth forth.

Lines 5 10 then explains how different the African culture is from the Western culture. Lines 5 -7 describe the African culture as tender but sometimes tenuous. It also takes note of its crudeness and the African traditional religion that is characterized by blood scarified. Then in line 8 10, the Western culture introduced by colonialism comes with technology and development new languages (English and French) and makes most Africans act outside the norms of the land, making them outlaws.

In stanza two, the poet persona chooses not to think of the effects of colonialism on our culture, as negative. Instead, he thinks it is time that the African culture is refined and developed and there is no better way than to use the culture already imposed on us for the betterment of ours. Thus, he pleads that those with vast knowledge of the African heritage (our fathers) should sew the old days for us to we can wear them under our new garments. This way, the African culture will always be part of our lives, though we have been made to don the gown of westernization. He claims that we have gotten introduced to a lot of these foreign culture but as we choose to ignore their overwhelming power we do not ignore completely but we take some useful parts of this culture and apply it with ours. This interaction will bring forth a new life and beginning for us and we will revel in our moments of transformation as described in line 18-21.

2.     CULTURAL ASSIMILATION: In the poem, the Western Culture is accepted as being here to stay, so it is adopted by Africans. However, our African culture is not thrown out but we keep it to 'wear it' under our new garments.

3.     METAPHOR: This is used throughout the poem, especially to described the feeding away of one culture and making way a new and accepted.



THE SCHOOL BOY BY WILLIAM BALKE

I love to rise in a summer morn,

ANALYSIS

School Bay is a poem about the dilemma of a child who has to attend classes, though he doesn't want to. In stanza one, the boy wakes on a summer morning and he loves it. He loves to hear the birds sing to him on nearby trees and the horn of the huntsman is pleasant to his ears, all these being good company to him.

Stanza two, on the other hand isn't very exciting for the boy as he must go to school. The thought of this alone saddens him and it doesn't get any better as he is always under the watchful eye of the strict teachers which is in itself tiring. As a result, the day is not bright for all the children as they day is dull and full of dismay.

The boy turns to his parents in stanza five and asks them the obvious. He asks that if buds of plants are ripped and their blossoms blown away, causing plants not to bear fruits and stopping their flowers from beautifying the summer day, the summer will not be beautiful and refreshing, instead, it will emit a bleak and mellow aura, uncharacteristic of a summer day.

Now, in the last stanza, the boy asks how what has been ruined will ever be fixed. He asks how it will be possible to make a happy adult out of a child who has been stripped of every chance of happiness and childhood glee.

In line 14, the school boy will enjoy learning only, if it takes place in a 'bower' which is beautiful, surrounded by nature and usually used for relaxation.


3.     DISMAY: All through the six stanzas of the poem, the mood of the poet persona is dull and he is unhappy as he is being confined to the four walls of a classroom just like a bird.

CROSSING THE BAR BY ALFRED TENNYSON

Sunset and evening star

And one clear call for me!

And may there be no meaning of the bar,

When I put out to sea


But such a tide as moving seems asleep,

Too full for sound and foam,

When that which drew from out the boundless deep

Turns again home.


Twilight and evening bell,

And after that the dark!

And may there be no sadness of farewell,

When I embark;


For through from out our bounce of time and place

The flood may bear me far,

I hope to see my pilot face to face

When I have crossed the bar.


ANALYSIS

The poem is about death that marks the end of one's life. The poet persona however, is not hysteric about the imminent death but accepts it as a passage to a new phase where he hopes to meet his pilot who brings and takes him from the world.

Stanza one beings with 'sunset and evening star' a phrase which symbolizes the end of one's life, just like a day ends. Then at this time of the persona's life, he hears a call summoning him into the great beyond. He knows for sure his end has come he urges that there be no moaning of the bar', meaning he will have a smooth passage to death.

He continues in stanza two, explaining how he wants a quiet exit as he returns to his home after death. This belief is present in the Christian religion where it is believed that humans come from God and will return to Him at the end of their lives.

In stanza three, the twilight and evening bell announces his departure from this life, marking the finality of his stay on earth as darkness comes to reside. He urges that there should be no mourning, sadness or sorrow as he departs the world because, just then he will embark on another journey to eternity. This journey as recorded in the last stanza will lead to a place where he is not hindered by time or place, a place known as eternity and where he wishes to see his Pilot that is his maker (God or Jesus) face to Face once he leaves the world.

THEMES

1.     DEATH: The poem is all about death. Sunset, evening star, twilight, evening bell, farewell and dark, all signify the end of one's life. However in the poem, death does not have the power of finality. Though life ends on earth, it continues to the great beyond known as eternity, meaning there is life after death. Also, normally at a person's death, people mourn as they bid their farewell, but the persona urges that they be not sad as he is on the journey to meet his creator.


2.     LIFE AFTER DEATH: Stanza five of the poem describe the life the poet persona will live in the great beyond. Though there is darkness after death, light will shine on him again as he crosses the bar.


3.     ACCEPTANCE OF DEATH: The persona knows that his death is fast approaching but he doesn't panic. Instead, he accepts that death is inevitable and chooses to concentrate more on what awaits him in the life after death.


POETIC DEVICES

1.     SYMBOLISM: In the poem, there are some words which are used to mean something different from their literal meanings, Evening star and sunset in line 1 symbolize the last moments of person's life while twilight and evening bell in line a symbolize death.


2.     PERSONIFICATION: This poetic device transfers human qualities to inanimate objects or animals. In line 3 and 7, there is use of personification.


3.     ALLITERATION: it is the repetition of initial consonant sounds in a line of poetry. In line 2, 'c' alliteration in 'clear cut'.

4.     METAPHOR: It is a comparison without the use of 'as' or 'like'. The title of the poem crossing the Bar stands as a metaphor being compared to death.


THE PULLEY BY GEORGE HERBERT

When God at first made man,

Having a glass of blessings standing by,

"Let us" said he, "pour on him all we can"

Let the world's riches which dispersed lie,

Contract into a span.


So strength first made a way,

Then beauty flowed, then, wisdom, honour, pleasure,

When almost all was out, God made a stay,

Perceiving that alone of all his treasure,

Rest in the bottom lay.


"For if I should" said he,

"Bestow this jewel also on my creature,

He would adore my gifts instead of me,

And rest in Nature, not the God of Nature,

So both should lovers be.


'Yet let him keep the rest,

But keep them with repining restlessness;

Let him be rich and weary that at least,

If goodness lead him not, yet weariness

May toss him to my breast.


ANALYSIS

The poem narrates how God creates humans with a little bit of "this and that". The poem is exactly like the creation story recorded in the first chapters of Genesis in the Bible. God gives some things to man and withholds some, with the intention that either what He has given or what which He doesn't give, will draw them closer to the Lord.

In stanza one, God is not alone as He said 'Let us'. He calls the other heavenly beings so that they can all contribute to the creation of man. And in this creation, He has blessings and riches in the mixture that makes man.

In stanza two, so many bestowments followed such as strength, beauty, wisdom, honour and pleasure. All these are present in the creation of the humans God has created. He gave man all these but in the end, He withholds the 'rest'.

He pauses in stanza three and explains why He will not bestow rest on man. If He bestows all He has in store for man, then, there will be adoration and worship of these things, instead of Him who gives them. So instead of man finding comfort in God who has give them all these gifts, He will seek his rest in all these things of nature and abandon God.

In the last stanza, God finalizes His decision to give both blessings and weariness as He is sure that if man does not turn to him in thanks for all the blessings, then he sure will when he is faced with life's difficulties.


POETIC DEVICES

1.     ALLUSION: This is a reference to a past event or historical happenings in a literary work. In the poem, reference is made to the creation story in the Bible and this is known as Biblical allusion.


THEMES

1.     CREATION OF MAN: The poem centers on how God creates man. In creating man, God put in a lot of work, adding blessings, riches and beauty. He also refused to add some things according to His will.


2.     GOD BEING OMNIPOTENT: The poem also describes the unlimited power and authority which God has over all His creation, including man. He chooses to create us and He can do so the way He likes. To some, He has given beauty and to some He has not. In all, He has refused everyone rest so that their life be full of restlessness and weariness. Even as He does this, no one can question him. He also reserves this right so that we can all reverence him and always find our way to him no matter how much we have achieved in life.


3.     Vanity: In the poem, there is emphasis on the importance of rest, comfort and satisfaction. If this is absent in one's life, all the wealth wisdom, beauty or accomplishment he possess will all be futile, with no value. God knows the importance of this so He has refused to bestow it upon man, as a form of control over his creation.



THE PROUD KING BY WILLIAMS MORRIS

ANALYSIS

The poem is about the journey an arrogant king has to make through life in order to realize that he is no different from everyone and a poor person can become wealthy and vice and versa in an instant. He rules as a dictator and no one can question his orders. He has so ruled from a young age till he now has grey hair and this makes him even more arrogant, considering himself greater than his fathers that have ruled before him and achieved nothing near what he has achieved on the throne. He wakes up one morning and starts to think of his great achievements and comes to the conclusion that he is such a great king that he may not die like his predecessors have.

One day, he makes a journey into the forest to hunt and he chases an animal deep into his forest on his strong horse, leaving his servants behind. He gets to a river and realizes he has lost track of the animal. Then, he decides to take a swim. He takes off his clothes and removes his crown from his head, making him just like a common and unrecognized by anyone as the king. He finishes his swim and discovers all his cloth and crown are gone and he starts shouting, thinking his servants can hear him. After futile shouts, he gives up and heads out naked, wandering around the forest. He decides to head out to the house of one of his rangers who takes care of the king's forest but when he gets there, the ranger doesn't recognize that he is the king. He reveals that he is King Jovinian but the ranger Hugh rebukes him for wanting such a high position when he is just a lowly poor man. Hugh asks that Jovinian be given a coat to cover his nakedness and some food to eat but the king becomes angry that Hugh does not treat him like the king he is. He becomes angry, swears that Hugh will not go unpunished as he will order that his house be burnt. After saying all this, he runs back into the forest, still naked.

While all this is happening, someone who disguises himself as the king steals the king's horse, clothes and crown and returns to the king's servant as king Jovinian. No one sees through the façade and he is escorted back to the palace to reign as their king.

Back in the forest, Jovinian makes his way to a path way and meets the entourage of a Councilor named Duke Peter. The Councilor also doesn't recognized him but calls him a mad man and a fool advising him not to go into the city spreading the tale that he is the king. In the process, one of Duke Peter's servants hit Jovinian with a sword, an act which further emphasis that he has been reduced to nothing. Duke Peter gave him some money and continues on his own journey.

Jovinian, alone on his journey of transformation reminisces on his past life of wealth, reverence and authority. He falls asleep and wakes up forgetting all that all that has happened. He later remembers and tries to find a solution to his problem. He makes his way to the city where he is once king but instead of telling people he is the king, he tells that he is a merchant who has been robbed of all he has. He meets a man and they become friends. He asks that he should be directed to the palace where he knows a squire who can help him, promising to repay this friend in the future. Thus, his friend introduces him to a man named Christopher a-Green who leads him to the palace.

All through their way to the palace, Jovinian tries to hide himself from the people, lest they recognize him in his tattered state. He gets to the palace but instead of his servants welcoming him, they think he is mad and refuses to allow him be presented to his council. At this point, Jovinian becomes sober and sad as he looks upon his soldiers and servants who bow down to him in humility and reverence him two days earlier, only to rebuke and ridicule him because they no longer recognize him as king.

He is later brought before the man who poses as king in his place. Jovinian starts to talk angrily, accusing the fake king to be an imposter but no one believes him. The imposter counters Jovinian and asks Jovinian's wife who the real king is. To Jovinian dismay, his queen kisses the imposter's feet and all his Counselors all think the same. Even his dog which has accompanied him on numerous hunting expeditions doesn't recognize him. Then, the imposter asks that he accepts him as king or he will be made his servant but Jovinian is adamant. With this, he is dragged on the streets and thrown out of the gate.

Jovinian visits the house of a hermit who used to give him counsel as a king. The hermit too doesn't recognize him and asks that instead of spinning a lie about being king, he should ask for physical or psychological help.

All this becomes too much for Jovinian and he breaks down and cries. He pleads to God that he should have mercy upon him and forgive all his wrongs that have been committed through acts of pride. He proclaims that he recognizes and acknowledges God as the master of all men and pleads that his position be restored. The hermit hearing all this now believes Jovinian is the king and offers him an ass to convey him back to the palace. An ass unlike a horse, also signifies humility. He sets out to ride to the palace and the guards greet him with no hostility, as the imposter has already informed them of his coming. He gets into the palace and sees his queen sleeping next to the image of the king. He gets angry but he sees the appearance of the imposter is sparkling like that of a heavenly being-an angel. Immediately, he realizes that God knowingly makes him go through all this to make a point. The angel then explains to him how the Lord has made everyone unable to recognize him so that he will recognize the Lord as the greatest person in all the world. The angel also advises him to remain in humility as he has been given a second chance unlike Lucifer who was not allowed to repent before being punished. The angel also predicts the end of the world where the world will be destroyed and the judgment of the Lord will come upon all men. Lastly, he hints that Jovinian will have a place beside the Lord in heaven.
