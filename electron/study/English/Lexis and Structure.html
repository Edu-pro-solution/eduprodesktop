TENSES


The various forms of the present tense are

The Present Simple Tense

It is used for

·     Regularly occurring events. Examples

·     I brush my teeth daily

·     She goes to church on Sundays

·     He often sees me

·     Expressions that show general truth. Examples:

·     Chickens lay eggs

·     Accidents are caused by carelessness

·     Events that show near future

·     Peter comes home tomorrow

·     The plane leaves in an hour's time

·     Events which are seen in the way they occur. Examples

·     In a sport report Remi passes the ball to Lola. Lola catches it, throws it into the basket and gets a score.

·     Stage direction Sadiku completes her part, nods and goes out

The Present Continuous Tense

It is used for an event that progress in the present and not yet completed. It takes the auxiliary verb 'be' to make it presentable. It is used for the following:

·     An action that is in progress now. Examples:

·     I am working on my dress

·     We are changing the tyres

·     An action that occurs frequently. Examples:

·     Peter is coming home next week

·     Tolu is graduating soon.


The Present Perfect Simple Tense

It indicates a past action which either continues into the present or directly affects the present. It usually makes use of the auxiliary verb 'have' to show its presentation.

It is used in the following ways:

- To show a past action or series of past events which continue into the present. Examples:

     * I have owned a car since 1990 and I still do

     * He has often been warned but he won't improve

- A past event that is affecting the present. Examples:

* The rain has stopped (I can now go out)

* Oil stations have stopped supplying fuel. (It is now restricting people's movement).


Past Tenses

Simple Past Tense

It shows actions started and completed on the past. Examples:

·     The man failed the exam.

·     I liked the dress

Past Continuous Tense

It is used to express actions that were going on in the past, at a particular time or those actions in the past interrupted by another one. Examples:

·     We were running for dear life two hours ago

·     I was cleaning the house when I slipped and fell

Past Perfect Tense

It is used to show two or more actions that took place in the past, which one happened or was completed before the other. Examples

·     The train had left before I remembered

·     By the time I arrived home, my mum had travelled

Past Perfect Continuous Tense

It is formed with had, the word been and the ing from of the verb.

·     It is used in conditional clauses when the main clause contains should/would/might/could have. Example:

·     If they had been standing there, would have been killed


Future Tense

This tense is used to talk about future time. No change occurs in the simple form of the verb in the expression of the future but there are few ways of indicating futurity.

·     By using the mortal auxiliary verbs shall and will. Examples

I shall travel soon

Tolu will see the manager when he arrives

To express simple futurity, shall is normally used with the first person, while will is usually used with the second and third person.

·     By using the form be going to and infinite. Example

Akin is going to succeed

It is going to rain today

We are going to defeat them in the game

·     By using the present progressive and an expression indicating the future. Examples:

Adamu is learning tomorrow

We are taking the examination next week

·     By using the simple present and an expression indicating the future: Examples

The bus leaves tomorrow

The plane arrives tonight

Here, we have 'the baby' because mention has already been made of 'a baby' in the first sentence.

- The following highlight how 'the' is used with proper nouns in English language.

Intensifiers

These are adverbs used with adjectives or other adverbs to make their meanings better understood. They include the following:

·     Too Very, Not Very

'Too' indicate that something is so big heavy hot, etc. that it causes a problem. It is used in a negative sense to suggest a higher degree tan is desired (i.e. excess). Examples:

* The skirt is too short

* The school is too far

'Very' is used to say that something is big, heavy, hat, etc to a high degree. It can be used with or without not. Examples:

* The clothes are very cheap

* She is not very sick


·     Quite, Not Quite

Quite has two meanings

* Completely Examples:

i. This bucket is quite empty

ii. This bucket is not quite empty

* Family i.e. to a certain degree

Note: when quite means fairly, 'not' should not be used before it. Examples:

i. It is quite hot

ii. They were quite polite

·     Enough     

This means sufficient or sufficiently. It usually comes after an adjective or adverb. Examples:

- Is that chair big enough for you?

- She is old enough to be your mother.


·     Very, Much And Far

'Very' can be used before much before far. Examples

* This work is very much easier than she thought

* The work is much (or far) easier than she thought.


·     Rather

This emphasizes the meaning of the adjective or adverb it goes with. Usually it does not occur with a negative: Examples

* She is rather lazy

* She is rather lazier than I thought.


·     Such And So

They have similar meanings

Note: when 'such' is used with a singular countable noun, it is followed by the article 'a'. Examples:

* What a bountiful harvest. I can't remember when we had such a great harvest of wheat.

* I have never seen such big bananas before


·     Fairly and To Some Extent

'Fairly' means quite

Examples:

* He is fairly short

* His lecture was fairly long

'To some extent' discloses the meaning to 'fairly'but it is only used with verbs.

Examples:

* His friend has improved to some extent

* She has succeeded to some extent


·     Intensifiers In Clusters

Sometimes, intensifiers combine to form expressions. Here are some examples:

Rather too much food;

A little too old to stand;

Quite so young;

Quite a lot more money;

Much too thirsty,

Quite a few dancers, and

Rather too slowly etc.


Comparatives

* Latter and last refers to position or order and are opposed to former and first respectively, both former and latter refer to two persons or things only;

- The latter part of his book is more interesting than the former

- Ejike was the last man to leave the room.

* Farther means 'more distant' and further means 'additional', but this distinction is not always observed;

- Abah-Unor is farther from Lagos than Benin city.

- He said that further discussion was necessary.

* Few denotes number and is always plural while little denotes size, degree or quantity and is both singular and plural

- Few men are really happy in Nigeria nowadays

- Little children are innocent

- A little milk is necessary

* Outer refers to position, utter refers to degree

- The circumference of the outer circle is one kilometre

- This is a matter of utter disgrace

* When two qualities in the same person or thing are compared, more is used instead of '-er' in order to form the comparative.

- This book is cheaper than good (wrong)

- This book is more cheap than good (right)

* In a comparison between objects of the same class, such restrictive words as other or else, should be used

- Akin and Nas are greater than all other authors

- I like rice better than everyone else in class

- Chike is taller than anyone else in my class

* In stating an object as the supreme member of its class, that class should not be restricted by the use of any, other or else.

- Soyinka is the greatest of all other Nigerian poets (wrong)

- Soyinka is the greatest of all Nigerian poets (right)

* If one of the two adjectives qualifying the same noun is compared, the other adjective should also be compared

- This is the safest and brilliant of all the plans (wrong)

- This is the safest and most brilliant of all the plans (right).

* When two persons or things are compared, the comparative degree is used, but if more than two performs or things are compared, the superlative degree is used;

- Akky is much the cleverer boy of the two

* Such adjectives as absolute, complete, entire, excellent, extreme, full, ideal, perfect, preferable (to), round, square, unique and universal carry in themselves sufficient superlative force and do not need further comparison or quantification.

- My cup is more preferable than yours (wrong)

- My cup is preferable to yours (right)

PARTS OF SPEECH


Preposition

A preposition is a word whose main function is to relate a noun or a pronoun to another word in the sentence. The preposition is said to govern a noun or a pronoun following it.

Examples:

i. The women in the car are singing

ii. The boys were swimming in the river

Types of Prepositions

1.     Single or Simple Preposition: These include words like near, above, about, through, across, against, under, outside, up, off, in, with, towards, among, over, within, inside, around, below, at, until, into, etc.

2.     Complex or Grouped Prepositions: These are, out of, according to, by way of, in regards to, with respect to, up to, inspire of, in place of, because of, as far as, away from, instead of, etc.

Classifications of Prepositions

a.     Positional prepositions: These prepositions show the position of a thing or person in relation to another thing or person.

Examples;

i.     He is standing on the chair

ii.     The girl ran across the street

Position preposition also include 'above', 'beside', beneath, below, against etc.

b.     Direction Prepositions: These go with verbs of motion. They indicate the direction of movements

Examples;

i.     Mary climbed up the hill

ii.     Mercy walked down the valley.

Other examples of direction prepositions include towards, along, unto, past, to etc.

c.     Time Prepositions: These express time examples include before noon, after noon, during the meeting etc.

d.     Prepositions that express instrumentality. These are 'through hardship', 'with daggers', 'by my wife' etc.

e.     Manner prepositions: These express manner.

Examples include: 'with hatred', 'withfortitude etc.

f.     Purpose Prepositions: These give us the purpose for something. Example: For loves sake, for knowledge, for peace.

g.     Destination Prepositions: Like the direction preposition, they also go with verbs of motion. Destination prepositions usually make reference to a given point 'to' or 'from' a destination. Examples include "to", "from", "into", and "out of"

Examples

1.     My sister went to Enugu.

2.     I saw brother going to Lagos yesterday.

Nouns

Noun is often called a naming word, that is to say that nouns are naming words in a sentence or sentences. Nouns are very common feature of language and they are categorized into various classifications as follows: Common noun, proper noun, concrete noun, abstract noun, countable and uncountable noun etc.


Countable Noun

A countable noun, also known as a count noun is a noun that can be preceded by the world 'a' and can exist on a plural form. When the plural form is used, it is not usually preceded by a determiner, but it is used alone e.g city (plural cities), gate (plural gates), foot (feet), hat (hats), lady (ladies), monkey (monkeys) etc.


Uncountable Noun

An uncountable noun, also known as uncount noun, is a noun that cannot usually be preceded by the word 'a' or 'an' and does not usually exist in a plural form. Abstract nouns tend to be uncountable. The following are examples of uncountable nouns. Poverty, luggage, information, sugar, education, petrol, bread, equipment, baggage etc.


Proper Noun

Proper noun refers to the specific names of persons, cities, town, countries, continents and instruments and institutions, months and days. These include: Bolanle, Anu, Yusuf, Joy, Wale, Nigeria, Africa, Asia, March, January, Central Bank of Nigeria etc. A proper noun must always begin with Capital letters.


Common Noun

This refers to a general name of things, concepts, animals and ideas. Words such as teacher, boys, town, hotel etc. are common nouns. This is because they are not the particular names by which the people and things being described are called A common noun needs not begin with a capital letter unless when it begins a sentence.

Collective Noun

A collective noun is a singular noun that refers to a group of things or people when the while the underlined words are collective nouns.

·     A pack of wolves

·     A flock of sheep

·     A herd of cattle

·     A team of players

·     A swarm of flies

Abstract Noun

Abstract nouns are things or concepts that cannot be touched or seen, they exist in our imagination that is, we know they exist by experience and feeling (emotions).

These are nouns like anger, beauty, happiness, fear, Christianity, wisdom, courage, idea, death, etc.


Concrete Noun

A concrete noun refers to something that you can see and touch. All other types of nouns [common, proper and collective] except abstract nouns are concrete nouns because they can be seen and touched.


Verbal Noun

When the present participle functions as a noun, it is known as a verbal noun or gerund. The verbal noun is so called because although formed with a verb, it functions as a noun acting as the subject of a sentence.

For example:

·     Smoking is bad for your health

·     Stealing is a crime

·     Jogging is a good exercise


Pronouns

Clauses

A clause is a group of word s with a finite verb, forming a sentence or part of sentence. It usually has a subject and predicate but unlike sentences, not all clauses can stand alone and make sense. There are two types of clauses; main or independent clause and subordinate or dependent clause.

Main clause

Min or independent cause is a clause that can stand alone and make sense. Examples.

·     He was at the office when I arrived

·     We took the train because the car broke down

Some sentences consist of more than one main clause connected by a coordinating conjunction such as 'and', 'but' or 'or'. Such sentences are known as compound sentences. Examples:

·     I was very angry and he knew it.

·     It was a sunny day but it was very cold.

Subordinate Clause

Subordinate or dependent clauses cannot stand on their own for a complete meaning; they rely on the main clause of a sentence. Examples:

·     Although he did not write the test, he passed the course

·     I bought some books when I travelled to London.

Note that subordinate clauses are introduced by a subordinating conjunction or a relative pronoun.

Figures of Speech






FIGURES OF SPEECH

Figures of speech are words used in a special way to show deeper meaning from its original form of presentation. They are:

·     Simile

This is a figure of speech that shows a comparison of two things or persons of different characteristics with the use of words such as 'as' or 'like'. Examples:

·     She talks like a parrot

·     He is as tall as a palm tree


·     Metaphor

This is a figure of speech that shows a direct comparison by naming a thing or person in the way it looks. It does not make use of 'as' or 'like' o show its presentation. Examples:

·     He is a chameleon

·     Shola is a mechanical slave


·     Personification

It deals with the giving of life to inanimate objects as if they were living. Examples:

·     The moon smiled at them last night.

·     Love is blind.


·     Irony

Is speaks the opposite of what is intended. Examples:

·     He steals and I like him

·     Driving while drunk is enviable

·     


·     Hyperbole

It presents an unpleasant fact in a mild/pleasant manner. Examples:

·     She is mentally imbalanced.

·     The man has gone to visit his ancestors.


·     Oxymoron

It shows the juxtaposition of two contracting words in an expression. Examples;

·     He is a little giant

·     The issue has become an open secret


·     Paradox

It shows a message which is contradicting and seems foolish but has a hidden truth when deeply examined. Examples:

·     The child is the father of the man

·     The pen is mightier that the sword


·     Rhetorical question

It asks a question which requires no answer because the answer is not necessary. It is usually used by a speaker to capture the attention of the audience while making points in a speech. Examples:

·     Where in the world will you find a place like home?

·     Can time ever wait for anyone?


·     Apostrophe

It addresses a thing or person not present as though it were present at the time of speaking. Examples:

·     Oh! Death, where is thy song?

·     O Wild West wind, thou breathe of autumn's being.


·     Litotes

This shows an idea that is emphasized by deliberate understatement using the negative of the opposite intention. Examples:

·     The woman is not lacking in beauty

·     He contributed in no small amount to the success of the party.

·     Metonymy

It shows the use of an idea or object to stand for something closely associated with it. Examples:

·     The guests were invited over to the table

·     The princes were involved in the race for the throne.


·     Synecdoche

It uses a part of a person, place or thing to represent the whole or uses the whole to stand for a part. Unlike metonymy, the part is an inseparable part of the whole. Examples:

·     Dinner was prepared for fifty mouths

·     The government should encourage the brains of the country.


·     Pun

It is simply referred to as a play on words. It plays on words which have similar sounds but different meanings.

Examples:

·     The minister claims that he is a soul administrator (pun on soul/sole)

·     The dead drunkard was laid on his beer (pun on beer/bier)


·     Alliteration

It is the repetition of consonants sounds at the beginning of words in expressions. Examples:

·     Let us sing a song of sixpence

·     Jane's junior just travelled


·     Assonance

It is the repetition of vowel sounds at the beginning of words in an expression. Examples:

·     Only old investors owe people

·     Everybody envies everything about Jude


·     Onomatopoeia

It uses words to form actual sounds made. Examples: zoom, splash, hiss, Ping-Pong, crash, cough, etc.
