LAWS OF CHEMICAL COMBINATIONS

These are the laws that explain the condition for two chemicals to be combined together. These laws were actually used by John Dalton to explain its atomic theories.

There are three laws of chemical combination, which allows

·     Law of Conservation of Matter/Mass

·     Law of Definite Proportion/Proust's law of Constant Composition

·     Law of Multiple Proportions

Law of Conservation of Matter: This states that matter can neither be created nor destroyed Butcan be transformed during a chemical reaction. This is the law which is applicable to the balancing of chemical equations.

E.g. CaCO3 → CaO + CO2

To verify this law, the reagents that should be used must not give a gaseous product or a violate product But one of the products can be precipitation.

Law of Definite Proportion: This states that all pure samples of a particular chemical compound obtained the same elements combined in the equation proportion by mass.

This law explains that irrespective of the volume of a particular chemical compound the mole ratio of the elements must be constant.

Let say for instance, there are two samples A and B of copper oxide.

In sample A: 3.2g of Copper combined with 0.8g of Oxygen.

In sample B: 6.4g of copper combined with 1.6g of Oxygen

Law of Multiple Proportion: This states that when two elements combined together to form more than one chemical compounds, the fixed mass of one which combine with several masses of the other, formed compounds which are in simple multiple ratio.

If P and Q are two different elements which can be combined together to form compounds PxQy and PxQz. It is observed that the mass of P is fixed while that of Q varies.

Practical examples include: FeCl2 and FeCl3, CuO and Cu2O, NO and N2O, SO2 and SO3etc.

For instance, there are two samples A and B containing oxides of copper.

In sample A: 3.2g of copper combined with 0.8g of Oxygen

Gay Lussac's Law of Combining Volumes

This is a law that explained the chemical behaviour of gases unlike other gas laws that explained the physical behaviour of gases.

This law actually explained the ratio of combination of volumes of gases when they react chemically to each other. Hence, the law states that when gases react, they do so in volumes which are simple ratio to one another and to the volumes of the products, if gaseous provided that temperature and pressure remains constant.

Under stoichiometry of a chemical reaction, it was said that only the moles of the reactants can be compared with respect to the product but not their reacting masses.

This is because they have different molar masses but in case of gases where all of them have the same molar volume at s.t.p, then their volumes can be compared to one another.

For instance, 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(g)

Mole ratio 2     1      2

Volume     xcm3 ½ x cm3 x cm3

Volume     50cm3     25cm3      50cm3

If it happens that the amount of one gas is more than the required volume, the volume left will be unreactive and hence, it is called Excess Reagent, while the other gas is called the Limiting Reagent.

Avogadro's Law: This is like a modification to the Gay Lussac's law of combining volumes.

A professor of chemistry known as Amedeo Avogadro explained the Gay Lussac's law of combining volume in a better way. Hence, "Avogadro's law states that equal volume of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain the same number of molecules".

From his experiment, the molar volume of a gas was determined to be 22.4dm3 per mole at standard temperature and pressure and also he said 1 mole of any substance contain 6.02 x 1023 number of particles.
