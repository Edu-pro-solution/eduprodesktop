CHEMICAL THERMODYNAMICS

This is an aspect of chemistry that deals with the interrelation between work and energy.

You can also view it as the combination of two terms "thermo" and "dynamic" of which thermos has to do with heat energy and dynamic has to do with change or you rather say motion.

Hence, chemical thermodynamics is an aspect of chemistry that deals with energy change during a chemical reaction.

In some cases, the study of heat change during a chemical reaction is termed Thermochemistry.

Let's consider these three basic terms: Universe, system; surroundings under chemical thermodynamics

Universe: This is a term given to the entire space i.e. both the system and the surrounding.

System: This is a part of the universe when the chemical reaction is taking place.

Surrounding: This is the part of the universe where there is no chemical reaction. It is the remaining part of the universe after which the system has been chosen.

It should be noted that systems can be classified into three depending on the interaction between the system and its surrounding. There are: Open system, Closed system and Isolated system.

Open System: A system is said to be opened when there is exchange of heat and matter between the system and its surrounding.

Closed System: A system is said to be closed if there is no exchange of matter between the system and its surrounding But there is exchange of heat.

Isolated System: A system is said to be isolated if there is exchange of neither matter nor heat.

Enthalpy of a Reaction

The term enthalpy is used to represent heat change of a chemical reaction. It is a well-known fact that during a chemical reaction, the product and the reactant can never have equal heat content. It is either the heat of the product is more or the heat of the reactant is more.

Consider the chemical equation below:

A + B → C + D

Here, A and B are the reactants and C and D are the products

A      +      B      →      C      +      D

          HR                    HP

Heat content of the reactant          Heat content of the product

So, HR ≠ HP

The reason for this is that a chemical reaction will either release heat to the surrounding or absorbs heat from the surrounding.

When, HR> HP heat is released

When, HP > HR heat is absorbed.

Hence, ⧍H which is the enthalpy of the reaction is simply the difference between the heat contents of the product and that of the reactant. It can be deduced that ⧍H can either be +ve or ve.

Under this heading, reactions are divided into exothermic and endothermic.

Exothermic Reaction

This is a reaction whereby heat is released to the surrounding which is as a result of the reactant having greater heat content and hence leads to a negative value of ⧍H

A      +      B      →      C      +      D

          HR                    HP

⧍H = HP - HR

Since HR> HP

⧍H = -ve

Hence, the following are properties of an exothermic reaction.

·     Heat is loss to the surrounding

·     Heat content of the reactant is greater than the heat content of the product

·     Enthalpy has a negative value

·     The wall of the container is warm to touch

Endothermic Reaction

This is a reaction whereby heat is absorbed from the surrounding which is as a result of the reactant having a lesser heat content and hence leads to a positive value of ⧍H.

Consider the chemical equation below:

A + B → C + D

Here, A and B are reactants and C and D are products.

A      +      B      →      C      +      D

          HR                    HP

Heat content of the reactant          Heat content of the product

⧍H = HP - HR

Since, HP> HR

⧍H = +ve

Hence, the following are the properties of an endothermic reaction.

·     Heat is absorbed from the surrounding

·     Heat content of the product is greater than the heat content of the reactant

·     Enthalpy has a positive value

·     The wall of the container is cold to touch

Calorimetry

In the laboratory, heat changes in physical and chemical processes are measured with the use of a calorimeter which is a closed container designed for this purpose. Hence, Calorimetry is a term given to heat change measurement of a process.

It should be noted that a calorie is a unit of heat energy which is equivalent to 4.2J on approximation. It is the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of 1g of water by 1oC.

Calorimetry cannot be done without the basic knowledge of heat capacity, specific heat capacity and molar heat capacity.

Heat Capacity

It is the quantity of heat required to raise the temperature of a body by 1oC or 1K. It is denoted with symbol "C" and measured in JK-1 or JoC-1

Law of Heat Exchange

This states that when two bodies of different temperatures are in contact, there is heat flow from the hotter body to the colder body until a state of thermal equilibrium is attained.

Thermal equilibrium is a state where the two bodies have equal temperature.

So, here Heat loss by body A = Heat gain by body B

If after which A and B are already in a state of thermal equilibrium and another body C is brought in contact with them with a higher temperature, t3 there will be heat flow to A and B from C until the three bodies attain a state of thermal equilibrium. This statement is called the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics.

Standard Enthalpy of Reaction

It is noted that standard enthalpy is different from enthalpy. Standard enthalpy implies that the react is done under standard conditions which are: 1atm pressure, 25oC temperature and 1 mole.

Recall, enthalpy is denoted as ⧍Ho with the superscript "o" represents standard state. So, standard enthalpy of reaction is ⧍Hro which is the amount of heat energy released or absorbed during a chemical reaction under standard condition.

Given: aA + bB → cC + dD as a chemical reaction, ⧍Hro = [c⧍Hfo (c) + d⧍Hfo (D)] - [a⧍Hfo (A) + b⧍Hfo (B)]

Standard Enthalpy of Formation (⧍Hfo)

This is the quantity of heat energy released or absorbed when 1 mole of a substance is formed from its constituent elements under standard conditions.

It should be noted that standard enthalpy of formation of free elements or diatomic gases is equal to zero. Except for those that exhibit allotropy. It is only the stable allotrope that has its ⧍Hfo to be zero, But for the unstable allotropes ⧍Hfo is not zero.

For Carbon: ⧍Hfo (graphite) = 0, ⧍Hfo (Diamond) = + 1.90KJmol-1

For Sulphur: ⧍Hfo (rhombic) = 0, ⧍Hfo (monoclinic) = -155.2 KJmol-1

It should be noted that in every reactions bonds are broken and also bonds are formed.

Indirect Method

In this case, several reactions are required to arrive at the product. In a nutshell, the Hess's Law of constant heat summation is applicable. The law was stated by Swiss chemist called Germain Henri Hess. They called him the father of thermochemistry for formulating the Hess's Law.

This law states that regardless the route taken by a chemical reaction, the enthalpy of the reaction is a constant provided that the initial condition is the same as the final condition.

By simple explanation, Hess's law made us to realise that enthalpy of a reaction is constant whether it takes a step, two steps, three steps or four steps.

For instance, A + B → F is the reaction of interest.

Then, A + B → C

C → D + E

D + E → F

This is a three steps reaction of A + B forming F.

If the heat of reactions are ⧍H1, ⧍H2 and ⧍H3.

Then, A + B → C, ⧍H = ⧍H1 + ⧍H2 + ⧍H3

A + B → C⧍H1

C → D + E ⧍H2

D + E → F, ⧍H3

When added, A + B → F      ⧍H

In some cases, you may need to reverse the reaction to take your desired product to product side or your desired reactant to reactant side. Also you may also need to divide through by a certain value to obtain your desired number of mole.

Rate Law

The rate law or rate equation for in chemical reaction is on equation that links the reaction rate with concentrations or pressures of reactants and constant parameters (normally rate coefficients and partial reaction orders). To determine the rate equation for a particular system one combines the reaction rate with a mass balance for the system. For a generic reaction aA + bB → C with no intermediate steps in its reaction mechanism (i.e., an elementary reaction), the rate is given by

R = K[A]x[B]y

Where [A] and [B] express the concentration of the species A and B, respectively (usually on moles per dm3)(molarity, m); x and y are the respective stoichiometric coefficients of the balanced equation; they must be determined experimentally. K is the rate co-efficient or rate constant of the reaction.

The value of this coefficient k depends on conditions such as temperature, ionic strength, surface area of the absorbent or light irradiation.

For elementary reactions, the rate equation can be derived from first principles using collision theory.

Again, x and y are not always derived from the balanced equation.

The rate equation is a differential equation, and it can be integrated to obtain an integrated rate equation that links concentrations of reactants or products with time.

This is the law we apply to write the equilibrium constant of a reaction at equilibrium. The law was stated by the chemists which are Guldberg and Waage (1865), building on Berthollet's ideas that for any reversible reaction to be at equilibrium that the rate of forward reaction must be equal to the rate of backward reaction.

The law of mass action states that the rate of a chemical reaction is directly proportional to the active mass of each of the reactants provided that temperature and pressure are kept constant.

According to this law the active mass of a reactant is considered to be the concentration of the reactant raised to its stoichiometric mole. But, if the reactant is in liquid or solid state the active mass is considered to be One (1).

Le Chatelier's Principle

Henry Louis Le Chatelier was an influential French chemist of the late 19th century. His work is based on the effect of factors affecting equilibrium position.

The principle states that when an external factor such as temperature, pressure or concentration is imposed on a reaction at equilibrium, the equilibrium position will shift so as to annul or neutralise the effect of the incoming constraint.

From this principle, it can be deduced that the three factors affecting equilibrium position are:

1. Temperature

2. Pressure

3. Concentration

It should be noted that catalyst is not among the factors that affect equilibrium position, But rather it reduces the time taken at which equilibrium will be attained. Hence, catalyst has no effect on both the equilibrium position and also the equilibrium constant. However, the importance of a catalyst cannot be left out for equilibrium reactions. They made the completion of the reaction more rapid than what it should be ideally.

In the Haber's process, finely divided Iron is used as a catalyst.

In the contact process, Vanadium V oxide is used as a catalyst.

Factors Affecting Equilibrium Position

It should be noted that there is difference between factor affecting equilibrium position and factor affecting equilibrium constant.

There are THREE factors affecting equilibrium position: Temperature, Pressure and Concentration.

But, it is only ONE factor i.e.temperature that affects equilibrium constant.

Temperature as a Factor Affecting Equilibrium Position

The temperature effect depends on the nature of the reaction. The reaction can either be exothermic or endothermic.

Consider the chemical reaction below as a reaction at equilibrium:

P + Q ᴝ R + S           ⧍H = -ve

The reaction above is exothermic to the forward path. Exothermic means it is losing heat to the surrounding so help such reaction temperature needs to be reduced for the forward reaction to be favoured. Hence, the equilibrium position shifts from left to right and leads to production of more R and S.

Then, the backward reaction is considered to be endothermic (i.e. needs heat energy from the surroundings). So, an increase in temperature will favour the backward reaction whereby leading to the shift of the equilibrium position from right to left with production of more of P and Q.

Let also consider the reaction below to be at equilibrium.

A + B ᴝ C + D           ⧍H = +ve     

The reaction above is endothermic to the forward path. Being endothermic means it is in need of heat from the surroundings, so an increase in temperature will favour the forward reaction by shifting the equilibrium position from left to right and thereby leading to the formation of more of C and D.

Here, the backward reaction is considered to be exothermic which means it loses heat to the surrounding. So, a decrease in temperature will favour the backward reaction such that equilibrium position shifts from right to left and thereby leading to more formation of A and B.

Hence, the effect of temperature on a reaction at equilibrium was summarized by a chemist called Vant Hoff in his law called the Vant Hoff's law of mobile equilibrium which states that for a reaction at equilibrium, an increase in temperature favours the side that is endothermic while a decrease in temperature favours the side that is exothermic.


Pressure as a Factor Affecting Equilibrium Position

The pressure effect depends on the number of gaseous molecules at either sides of the reaction.t

Consider the reaction below as a reaction at equilibrium.

aP + bQ ᴝ cR + dS

Hope you have not forgotten that a, b, c and d are called the stoichiometric mole. And here the stoichiometric mole is a very important factor to determine how reactions can be affected by pressure change.

If P, Q, R and S are all in gaseous state, then the pressure effect depends on the value of (a + b) and (c + d) respectively.

When (a + b) and (c + d) have the same value, such reaction cannot be affected by pressure change.

When (a + b) is greater than (c + d), an increase in pressure will favour the forward reaction such that equilibrium position is shifted from left to right and thereby leads to the formation of more of R and S respectively.

When (a + b) is lesser than (c + d), a decrease in pressure will favour the forward reaction such that the equilibrium position shifts from left to right and thereby leads to the production of more of R and S.

It is a vice-versa process, when an increase in pressure favours the forward reaction. Then, a decrease in pressure will favour the backward reaction.

Likewise, when a decrease in pressure favours the forward reaction. Then, an increase in pressure will favour the reverse reaction.

Consider the following equilibrium reactions:

1. N2(g) + 3H2(g) ᴝ 2NH3(g)

4 gaseous moles Vs. 2 gaseous moles

An increase in pressure will favour the forward reaction, while a decrease in pressure favours the reverse reaction.

2. N2O4(g) ᴝ 2NO2(g)

1 gaseous mole Vs. 2 gaseous moles

A decrease in pressure will favour the forward reaction, while an increase in pressure will favour the backward reaction.

3. H2(g) + Cl2(g) ᴝ 2HCl(g)

2 gaseous moles Vs. 2 gaseous mole

Here pressure will have no effect on this reaction.

4. HCOOH(aq) + CH3OH(aq) ᴝ HCOOCH3(aq) + H2O(l)

0 gaseous mole Vs. 0 gaseous mole

This reaction cannot be affected by pressure change.

5. CaCO3(s) ᴝ CaO(s) + CO2(g)

0 gaseous mole Vs. 1 gaseous mole

A decrease in pressure favours the forward reaction, while an increase in pressure will favour the reverse reaction. In summary, when there is increase in number of moles. Then, there will be decrease in pressure for the reaction to be favoured.

Concentration as a Factor Affecting Equilibrium Position

Here, addition and removal of the reactants and products will be used as a factor to know the position of equilibrium position.

Consider the reaction below to be in a state of equilibrium:

P + Q ᴝ R + S

When more of P or Q is added, it will leads to the formation of more of R and S which means the equilibrium position will shift from left to right in order to favour the forward reaction.

When either P or Q is removed, the backward reaction is favoured. This is because more of P and Q will be produced as they are removed. It will shift equilibrium position from right to left.

When more of R or S is added, the backward reaction is favoured, since they will be converted to more of P and Q as R and S are added. The equilibrium position shifts from Right to Left.

Also, when either R or S is removed, the forward reaction is favoured. This is because more of R and S will be produced as they are removed. It will shift equilibrium position from Left to Right.
