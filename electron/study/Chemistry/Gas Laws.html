Gas Laws








GAS LAWS

These are the theories that explain the behaviour of the gaseous particles:

1. Gas molecules move randomly on a straight line: This implies that gas molecules undergo rectilinear random motion which conformsto the phenomena supporting kinetic theory of matter.

2. The Cohesive force between the gas molecules is negligible. This implies that gas molecules cannot easily attract one another. It can also explain why gases do easily escape into the air.

3. The volume occupied by gas molecules is negligible compare to the entire volume of the container. This implies that the gas molecules expand to fill the container, But the actual space they occupy is usually very small. According to Van der Waal, it was explained using equation.

4. The collision between the gas molecules is perfectly elastic. This implies that when they collide both kinetic energy and momentum are conserved.

5. The temperature of the gas molecules is a measure of its average kinetic energy. This implies that the gas becomes more energetic when heated since they gain more velocity as a result of temperature increase.

But for better understanding of these laws, the following physical parameters of gas must be well-understood Volume, pressure and temperature majorly.


Volume

This is the amount of space occupied by the gas molecules. This is usually taken as the volume of the gas container. It is denoted with "V" measured in m3, cm3, dm3, litres and so on.

1 litre = 1 dm3 = 1000 cm3 = 0.001m3


Pressure

This is a measure of the amount of the collision between the gas molecules with one another and with the wall of the container.

The force per unit area exerted by the gas molecules on one another and the wall of the container. It is denoted with "P" measured in Nm-2, Pa, cm of Hg, mm of Hg, torr, Bar and so on.

1atm = 1.01 x 105Nm-2 = 1.01 x 105Pa = 760mmHg = 76cmHg = 760torr

The above pressure values are referred to as standard pressure of a gas.


Temperature

This is the degree of hotness or coldness of the gas or the measure of the average kinetic energy of the gas molecules. It is denoted with "T" and measured in oC, K, oF, oR.

Note that 0oC = 273K = 32oF = 0oR in the lower fixed point. 100oC = 373K = 212oF = 80oR upper fixed point.

The temperatures of the lower fixed point are referred to as standard temperature.

Avogadro's Law

This law was stated by an Italian physicist called Amedo Avogadro. He explained the relationship between the volume of a given mass of a gas and the number of molecules.

Hence, Avogadro's law states that equal volume of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contains the same number of molecules.

It can be otherwise stated that the volume of a given mass of a gas is directly proportional to the number of moles of the gaseous molecules, provided that temperature and pressure are kept constant.

It should be noted that equal mass of gases will not occupy the same volume at constant temperature and pressure. They only have equal volume when they have the same number of moles.

For instance, 1g of Hydrogen gas will occupy the same volume as 16g of Oxygen gas at standard temperature and pressure.

1g of H2(g) is ½ = 0.5moles

16g of O2(g) is 16/32 = 0.5moles

Since, they have the same number of moles, then they will occupy the same volume.

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressure

This law was stated by an English chemist called John Dalton in 1801 to explain the behaviour of gaseous mixture towards pressure.

Hence, Dalton's law of partial pressure states that for a mixture of gases which do not react chemically together, the total pressure exerted is the sum of the partial pressures of the individual gases that make-up the mixture. This is only true for only ideal gasesBut not real gases.

And each of these gases will have its individual number of moles, nA, nB and nc respectively. Then, the total numbers of moles, nT = nA + nB + nC

Likewise the volume, VA, VB and VC represent the volume of the individual gases. Then, total volume, VT = VA + VB + VC.
