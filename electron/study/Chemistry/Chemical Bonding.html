Chemical Bonding









CHEMICAL BONDING AND SHARE OF MOLECULES

It is a well-known fact that elements are chemically combined to form a compound. In every compound, there must be at least two elements joined together. The joining is actually done by a bond.

Hence, a bond is defined as an electrostatic force which holds two or more elements together in a given compound. An element goes into chemical bonding so as to attain a stable duplet or an octet configuration. It should be noted that only the group O elements on the periodic table are said to be in a state of duplet (i.e. for Helium) and octet for others. So, for this to be achieved by other elements, they have to go into chemical bonding.

For instance, the purpose of Sodium (Na) coming together with Chlorine (Cl) in Sodiumchloride (NaCl) is to attain stable octet configuration each. Likewise, Hydrogen (H) and Fluorine (F) in Hydrogen Fluoride (HF) are so that Hydrogen attains stable duplet configuration while Fluorine attains stable octet configuration.

Majorly, there bonds are divided into two, which are the strong bonds and the weak bonds. The strong bonds as said are electrovalent bond, covalent bond and dative bond. While the weak bonds are Hydrogen bond, metallic bond, Vander Waal forces and the London forces.

Electrovalent Bond

This is an electrostatic force that joins a cation to anion. This type of bond exists between a positively charged ion and a negatively charged ion. Hence, it is otherwise called anIonic Bond.

Electrovalent bond or ionic bond is the bond that exists between a metallic element and a non-metal element. In short, any chemical compound that has the ability to ionize such that the cation is not H+possesses electrovalent bond/ionic bond.

This is the strongest kind of bond that exists in a compound since it has to do with the attraction of two unlike charges.

In electrovalent bonding, there is usually the transfer of electron(s) from the metallic element to the non-metallic element so that they can both attain stable octet or duplet configuration or better still transfer of electrons from the positive ion to the negative ion.

In this type of bonding, it is usually noticed that the two participants do have wider difference in their electronegativity values. The positive partner with low electronegativity value and the negative partner with a large electronegativity value.

The following are the properties of electrovalent compound.

- They have high boiling and melting points. This is due to the fact that the bonds are usually strong and do require enough heat energy to be broken.

- They are soluble in polar solvents such as water. This is due to their high solubility power

- They are used as electrolytes. This is because of their ionization ability

- Their bonds are considered the strongest. This is due to the positive-negative attraction.

- They are usually in solid states at normal temperature.

Covalent Bond

This is an electrostatic force that join two non- metallic elements whereby each of the participating atoms donate an electron each to form the shared pair of electrons. This type of bond exists in a compound in which both atoms are in need of electron(s) to attain stable octet or duplet configuration.

For two atoms to go into covalent bonding, they must be in need of the same number of electrons. So, when there is only one shared pair of electrons, a single bond is formed ( - ), when there are only two shared pair of electrons, double bond is formed ( = ), and also if there are three shared pair of electrons, there will be triple bond ( ≡ ).

In this case, elements of the same atom can form covalent bond between itself. It is not a must for them to be from two different elements.

This bond is not as strong as the electrovalent bond, since it doesn't require attraction of opposite charges. Diatomic molecules such as H2, Cl2, F2, O2, N2 and so on are covalent compounds. Hence, their molecule are said to be line.

Hydrogen Bond

This type of bond exists when Hydrogen is covalently bonded with highly electronegative elements such as Oxygen, Fluorine, and Chlorine and so on. Due to high affinity of these mentioned elements for elections, they tend to attract the shared pair of electrons toward the nucleus of their own and away from that of Hydrogen. Hence, this makes Hydrogen to possess partial positive charge and the element partial negative charge.

Hydrogen bond can be used to explain high boiling points of compounds like HCl(aq), CH3OH(aq), CH­3COOH(aq) and so on .

Metallic Bond

Metallic bonding constitutes the electrostatic attractive force between the delocalized, electrons, called Conduction electrons and the positively charged metal ions.

Metallic bonding accounts for many physical properties of metals, such as strength, malleability, ductility, thermal and electrical conductivity, opacity and lustre.

Although, the term "metallic bond" is often used in contrast to the tem "covalent bond", it is preferable to use the term metallic bonding, because this type of bonding is collective in nature and a single "metallic bond" does not exist. Not all metals exhibit metallic bonding one such example is the Mercurous ion (Hg22+), which forms covalent metal-metal bonds.


Vander Waals Force

Vander Waals force (or Vander Waals interaction), named after Dutch Scientist Johannes Diderik Vander Waals, is the sum of the attractive or repulsive forces between molecules (or between parts of the same molecule) other than those due to covalent bonds or to the electrostatic interaction of ions with one another or with neutral molecules.

It should be noted that the Vander Waals force is the weakest of all the attractive forces and bonds. It is the force of attraction in discrete molecules, such as molecules that Sublime upon heating. Examples include: Ammonium Chloride crystals, iodine crystals, dry ice (solid Carbon IV Oxide), Camphor, Naphthalene and so on.

The Vander Waals force includes the following:

- Force between two permanent dipoles (Kees on force)

- Force between a permanent dipole and a corresponding induced dipole (Debye force)

- Force between two instantaneously induced dipoles (London dispersion force)

Vander Waals forces also define the solubility of organic substances in polar and non-polar media. They define the chemical character of many organic compounds.


London Forces

This is also known as Dispersion forces, London dispersion forces, instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces. It is a type of force acting between atoms and molecules. They are part of Vander Waals forces. The London force is named after the German-American physicist Fritz London.

London forces are exhibited by non-polar molecules because of the correlated movements of the electrons in interacting molecules. This is the only attractive intermolecular force present between neutral atoms (e.g. a noble gas). Without London forces, there would be no attractive force between noble gas atoms, and they wouldn't exist in liquid form.

London forces become stronger as the atom or molecule in question becomes larger. This trend is exemplified by the halogens (from smallest to largest: F2, Cl2, Br2, I2)
