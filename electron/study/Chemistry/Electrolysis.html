ELECTROLYSIS

Basically substances are divided into three classical states: Solid, Liquid and gas. A solid will conduct if it has free mobile electrons. A Sodium will conduct electricity ButSodium chloride will not. For liquid to conduct electricity, it has to be in aqueous form which means it must have free mobile ions. When Sodium chloride is dissolved in water; it has free mobile ions and hence, it conducts electricity. At room temperature gases are electrical insulators. But, they can be made to conduct when subjected to a high potential difference and a low pressure.

Electrolysis is defined as the chemical decomposition of a substance in aqueous or molten form when direct current is passed whereby a solution conducts electricity due to the presence of free mobile ions.


Terms associated with Electrolysis

These are terms used under electrolysis, and these terms must be well understood in order to be able the basic understanding of this topic.

Electrolytes and Non-electrolytes, voltameter, electrodes and electrolytic cell are the terms associated with electrolysis.

Electrolytes

These are substances in aqueous or molten form which allow electricity to pass through, while itself is decomposed at the end of the process. These substances are majorly electrovalent compounds which are dissolved in water. It can also be a Hydrogen bond containing compound. The ionization ability of the compound determines the strength of electrolyte. Electrolytes are divided intoStrong and Weak depending on the degree of dissociation.

Strong Electrolyte

This is any form of electrolyte that ionizes and dissociates completely in water. Examples are aqueous solution of strong acids (e.g. HCl, HNO3, H2SO4 ) aqueous solution of strong alkalis (e.g. NaOH, KOH) and aqueous solution of salts (e.g. NaCl(aq), CuSO4, ZnCl2, AgNO3, PbBr2, etc.)

Weak Electrolyte
This is a form of electrolyte that ionize partially in water. Examples are aqueous solution of weak acids (e.g. CH3COOH, H3PO4, H2CO3etc.), aqueous solution of weak bases (e.g. Ca(OH)2, BaO, etc.).

It should be noted that urine is a mixture of water and salts, hence, it is an electrolyte. So, it can be said that all forms of acidic solution, basic solution and salt solution are electrolytes.

Non-Electrolyte

These are substances in liquid state which not allow electricity to flow through them due to the absence of free mobile ions. These compounds are covalent compounds which are non-polar i.e. they can never have Hydrogen bond. Examples are Benzene, toluene, sugar solution, Ethers, Amine, and Amide and so on.

Conductivity of Electrolytes

The conductivity of an electrolyte depends on its strength and also its concentration. A strong electrolyte will conduct more than a weak electrolyte because a strong electrolyte will have more ions to carry the electric current.

Concentration of an electrolyte has inverse relationship with its conductivity. The more the concentration, the less the conductivity since an increase in concentration will not enable the ions to be able to move freely. So 2.00moldm-3 of NaCl(aq) will conduct more than 3.00moldm-3 of NaCl(aq). Also, a 2.00moldm-3 of NaCl(aq) will conduct more than a 2.00moldm-3 H3PO4(aq).

From the Kholraush's law of independent mobility of ions, the conductance of an electrolyte is the sum of the conductances of the cation and the anion which formed the electrolyte.

ΛNaCl = λNa+ + λCl-


Voltameter

This is a beaker-like material which is usually used as a container for the electrolyte. It is the container where the electrolyte is poured while setting-up the electrolytic cell. There is a special setting-up of the electrolytic cell. There is a special voltameter designed by a chemist called Hofmman for the electrolysis of acidified water (dil. H2SO4).

It is a form of H-like voltameter. But generally, voltameters are in form of beakers.

Electrodes

These are rod-like or plate-like materials which help in and out flow of electric current or electrons into the electrolyte. They are mostly metals that conduct electric current into the electrolyte or that conduct electric current out of the electrolyte. Electrodes are divided into: Cathode and Anode.

Cathode

This is a negatively charged electrode of an electrolytic cell which helps to conduct electric current out of the electrolyte. The word cathode is formed from the combination of cation and electrolyte.

They are electrodes that attract cations and d conduct electrons into the electrolyte which the cations take up to form the neutral metal. It is a well-known fact that gaining of electrons is reduction. Hence, reduction takes place at the Cathode.

Anode

This is a positively charged electrode of an electrolytic cell which helps to conduct electric current into the electrolyte. The word Anode is formed from the combination of anion and electrode.

They are electrodes that attract anions and conduct electrons out of the electrolyte which lossby the anion to liberate gases or given off by the anode to turn ions which go into the electrolyte.

As a result of the loss of electron, it can be concluded that Oxidation takes place at the Anode.

Ionization of Electrolyte

It is a well-known fact electrolytes are electrovalent compounds or Hydrogen bond- containing compounds. When an electrolyte ionizes, it gives a cation and an anion and also water also splits to H+ and OH-. If an electrolyte is represented as XY, Xn+ is the cation and Yn- is the anion.

So, Xn+ and H+ (from water) migrate to the cathode as the competing ions.

Also, Yn- and OH- (from water) migrate to the anode as the competing ions.

For instance, CuSO4(aq) when electrolysed will have Cu2+ and H+ are competing cations and SO42- and OH- are competing anions.

If ZnCl2(aq) is electrolysed, Zn2+ and H+ are competing cations and Cl- and OH- are competing anions. If HNO3(aq)­is electrolysed, H+ and H+ are competing cations and NO3- and OH- are competing anions.

If NaOH(aq)is electrolysed, Na+ and H+ are competing cations and OH- and OH- are competing anions.

It can be seen that, there are two cations that always migrate to the cathode and one of them is discharged and the other one is left in the solution. Likewise the two anions, one will be discharged and the other one is left into the solution.

The question is which one is to be discharged?

The answer to this can be treated under the ending of preferential discharge of ions.

Factors affecting Preferential Discharge of Ions

To know which of the ions to be discharged, there are three factors to be considered.

·     Position of the ions on the electrochemical series

·     Nature of the electrodes

·     Concentration of the electrolyte

Position of the Ions on the Electrochemical Series

The electrochemical series is of two kinds. For cations and anions respectively. For the cation, the ion below is preferentially discharged to the one above.

For the anion, the ion above is preferentially discharged to the ion below.

If this is the only factor to be considered, since OH- is one of the competing anion at the anode. It means OH- will surely discharge whereby leading to evolution of Oxygen gas.

4OH-(aq) → 2H2O(l) + O2(g) + 4e-

Also, H+ will be discharged in most of the case except if Cu2+, Hg22+, Ag+ or Au2+ is present together with H+ then the ion is discharged instead of H+. When H+ is discharged, Hydrogen gas is evolved at the cathode.

2H+ + 2e- → H2(g)

Every other cations are discharged as metal deposit on the cathode.

Xn+(aq)+ ne- → X(s)


Nature of the Electrode

In this case, electrodes which have the ability to react with the electrolyte are called an active electrode, and electrode will be active when the same metal made as the anode is dipped into its ionic solution. But a passive electrode is the one that do not react with the solution.

Anytime the electrode used as the anode is active, ions of the electrolyte will not be discharged. But part of the electrode goes into the solution as ions whereby the dissolved ions from the anode are deposited at the cathode.

If for instance, X-metal is the anode in X-ion solution (i.e. the electrolyte)

At the anode: X → Xn+ + n e-

At the cathode: Xn+ + n e- → X


Concentration of the Electrolyte

This is a measure of which of the two competing ions is more than the other.

Whenever the solution used is diluted, it is assumed the competing ions are in the same proportion since ionization of a water molecule is weak. If this happen position of the ion on the electrochemical series will be the factor to be considered to determine which ion should be discharged.

But, in a case where the ion of the electrolyte are more than H+ andOH- of water, a more concentrated ion will be discharged provided that the two competing ions are not too far apart from one another.

At most, three ions should be in between them. This happens when the electrolyte is concentrated solution or a fused solution.

It should also be noted that when you are asked to describe any electrolysis process, these three factors have to be considered. When the second factor is the case, the remaining two will not be considered. But is the electrodes are passive (e.g. Graphite, Platinum, Carbonetc.). The first and third will be considered only, however if the solution is diluted, it is only the first factor that will not be considered.


Question 1

Describe the electrolysis of dil. CuSO4 using copper cathode and platinum anode.

Answer

Electrolyte used → dil. CuSO4

Cathode used → Copper

Anode used → Platinum

Ionization of dil. CuSO4 and water (H2O)

CuSO4 → Cu2+ + SO42-

H2O → H+ + OH-

At the Cathode:

Cu2+ and H+ migrate to the cathode as the competing cations, since the solution is diluted, Cu2+ and H+ are in the same proportion. Hence, Cu2+ is preferentially discharged since it is below H+ on the series. Therefore, Copper is deposited at the cathode and the half equation is as written thus:

Cu2+ + 2e- → Cu(s) [reduction half equation]

At the Anode:

SO42- and OH- migrates to the anode as the competing anions, since the solution is diluted , SO42- and OH- are in the same proportion. So, OH- is preferentially discharged since OH- is above SO42- on the series. Therefore, Oxygen gas is liberated at the anode and the half-equation is as written thus:

4OH(aq)- → 2H2O(l) + O2(g) + 4e- [Oxidation half-equation]

The overall equation: 2Cu2+(aq) + 4OH(aq)- → 2Cu(s) + 2H2O(l) + O2(g)

It should be noted that the solution left in the Voltameter is H2SO4(aq) from the undischarged H+ at the cathode and undischarged SO42- at the anode.


Question 2

Describe the electrolysis of Brine using Carbon electrodes.

Answer

Electrolyte used → fused NaCl (Brine)

Cathode used → Graphite

Anode used → Graphite

Ionization of fused NaCl and H2O

NaCl → Na+ + Cl-

H2O → H+ + OH-

At the Anode:

Cl- and OH- migrate to the anode as the competing ions, since the solution is fused, Cl- will be discharged since it is more concentrated and not too far from OH-. Therefore, Chlorine gas is liberated at the anode.

2Cl-(aq) → Cl2(g) + 2e- [oxidation half equation]

At the Cathode:

Na+ and H+ migrate to the cathode as the competing ions, since the solution is fused, Na+ are more in the solution compare to H+. H+ will be discharged because Na+ is too far away from H+ despite the fact that Na+ is more concentrated. Therefore, Hydrogen gas is liberated at the cathode.

2H+(aq) + 2e- → H2(g)          [Reduction half equation]

The overall equation: 2H(aq)+ + 2Cl-(aq)→ H2(g) + Cl2(g)

The solution left is NaOH(aq) which is gotten from the combination of Na+ and OH- which are undischarged ions at the cathode and anode respectively.


Question 3

Describe the electrolysis of CuSO4(aq)using copper electrodes.

Answer

Electrolyte used → CuSO4

Cathode used → Copper Anode used → Copper

Ionization of CuSO4 and H2O

CuSO4- → Cu2+ + SO42-

H2O → H+ + OH-

At the Anode:

SO42- and OH- migrate to the anode as the competing ions, But it is unfortunate that none of them is discharged. Since the anode is an active electrode, part of the anode goes into the solution as Cu2+.

Cu(s) → Cu2+(aq)+ 2e-          [oxidation half equation]

At the Cathode:

Cu2+ and H+ migrate to the cathode as the competing ions, But it is unfortunate that none of them will be discharged, But the part dissolved at the anode is deposited on the cathode as copper.

Cu2+(aq) + 2e- → Cu(s)          [Reduction half equation]

The overall equation: Cu(s) + Cu2+(aq)→ Cu2+(aq)+ Cu(s)

Here, the concentration of the electrolyte is not altered at all, while the blue colour of the solution still retained.

Uses/Applications of Electrolysis

The following are the areas where electrolysis can be applied:

1. Extraction of highly reactive metals: The metals at the top of the electrochemical series such as Sodium, Potassium, Magnesium and Aluminium are extracted from their ore through electrolytic method.

2. Purification of Metals: When an impure metal is to be purified, the impure metal is made the anode. As the process progress, the impurities go into the solution provided that the solution contains the ions of the anode.

3. Electroplating of metals: Electroplating is a process whereby a metal with high resistance to corrosion is used to coat another metal. The metal to be coated is made the cathode, while the metal to be used for the coating is made to be the anode while the electrolyte is the solution of the ions of the anode.

4. Industrial preparation of gases. Gases like Hydrogen, Oxygen, Chlorine, bromine and many more can be produced through electrolysis process.

5. Preparation of chemicals, chemicals like HNO3, NaOH, KOH, H2SO4 and many more can be produced as the solution left after electrolysis.

6. It can be used for anodizing

7. It can be used to calibrate ammeter.

Electrode Potential

Whenever a metal is dipped into its ionic solution, there is always a potential difference which is set-up. The value of the potential difference depends on concentration of the solution, the temperature of the environment and the pressure.

These conditions have to be standardized to have a standard electrode potential of a metal ion/metal system. Electrode potential is denoted with E, when it is under standard condition, it is represented as Eθ are called Standard Electrode Potential

·     1moldm-3 of the metallic ion

·     25oC or 298K temperature of the environment

·     1atm or 760mmHg pressure

When the metal is dipped into a molar solution of its ion, there are two possible things that can be observed.

1. Some of the metals go into the solution as ions and leaving behind negative electrons which makes the plate to be negative and the solution to be positive.

2. Some of the metal ions in the solution are deposited on the plate which makes the plate to be positively charged and leaving behind the electrons in the solution.

Under the above standard conditions, metals which behave as (1) above have ve value of Eθ. But, metals which behave as (2) above have +ve value of Eθ.

1. This is experienced for metals above Hydrogen on the electrochemical series.     

Zn → Zn2+ + 2e-                         

2. This is experienced for metals belowHydrogen on the electrochemical series.          Cu2+ + 2e- → Cu

The S.H.E of Hydrogen is zero when done under standard conditions. Here, platinum plate is dipped into a solution of dilute H2SO4 which supplied the H+. This is written as H+/H2/Pt with Eθ = 0.00V. It is as represented in the diagram below:

When the two different metals dipped in their respective metal ions are connected together. What is formed is called Electrochemical Cellwhich converts chemical energy to electrical energy with the positive electrode being cathode and the negative electrode being anode. So, the potential difference obtained here is the electromotive force (e.m.f) of the cell formed.

     

E.m.f = Eθ(Cu2+/Cu) - Eθ(Zn2+/Zn)

Here, the system is Zn2+/Zn // Cu2+/Cu ; the // sign represent the salt bridge and / represent the partition between the metal and its solution.

Alternatively; E.m.f = Eθ(Cathode) - Eθ(Anode)

The salt bridge is paper soaked in NaCl solution which is used for charge balancing between the two solutions.

Electrochemical cells are divided into two types:

·     Primary cells          - Secondary cells

Primary Cells

These are electrochemical cells which cannot be recharged after discharge and moreso they discharge for short period of time. The major challenge about primary cells is the fact that they do face back e.m.f (i.e. they suffer polarization and local action). The polarization is the sudden drop in the voltage of the cell which is as a result of evolution of gases. This can be controlled by using a depolarizer. Also, local action occurs when the anode contains some impurities which set-up an internal circuit whereby no supply is done to the external cathode.

Examples of primary cells are simple voltaic cell or simple Galvanic cell, Leclanche' cell (wet and dry), the Weston cell and many more.



Secondary Cells

These are electrochemical cells which can be recharged after discharge and moreso do discharge over a long period of time. They do not face any form of back e.m.f, hence, they are far better compare to the primary cells.

Examples of secondary cells are Lead-acid accumulator, Nickel-Iron accumulator, Nickel- Cadmium accumulator and many more.
