FRICTION

Friction is the term which is used to describe the force that opposes the motion of a body. Friction acts opposite to the direction of motion of a body.

It should be noted that friction will not act on a body when there is no tendency for motion.

A.     Let us diagrammatically explain friction in a block of wood which is placed on a table and is about to be set in motion by a spring attached to it.

               

F: The force causing motion

Fr: Frictional force

R: Normal Reaction

W: Weight of the block acting downwards

1.     As the force F is exerted on the block from the spring balance, the block still remains at rest since an equal but opposite force of friction is acting at the underlying surface of the wood.

N.B: F = Fr --------(1)

2.     As the force increases in value, a point is reached where the block is just about to move the frictional force at this point is called the limiting frictional force.

What is limiting frictional force?

Limiting frictional force is the force frictional force that must be overcome for an object to just begin to move over another.

N.B: F = Fr = Fm --------(2) (No acceleration yet)

3. With increasing velocity, the block starts to move faster over the table; friction is still present even though the body is moving and the frictional force at this stage is called the "kinetic frictional force" or simply "kinetic friction"

It should be noted that the friction on the body when the body is at rest (static friction) is greater than when the friction on the body when the body is in motion.

Advantages of Friction

1.     Automobile tyres are made rough so that the brake can function properly

2.     Friction between our footwear and the ground enables us to walk without slipping

3.     Friction enables us to sharpen our tools

4.     Friction enables belt to move over pulleys

Disadvantages of Friction

1.     Friction causes heating in machines thereby reducing efficiency in machines

2.     Friction causes wear and tear

Laws of Solid Friction

1.     Friction acts so as to oppose motion

2.     Friction is independent of the surfaces in contact

3.     Friction depends on the nature of surfaces in contact

4.     Friction depends on the normal reaction
