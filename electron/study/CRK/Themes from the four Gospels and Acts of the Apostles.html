The Four Gospels and Acts of Apostles







1. THEMES FROM THE FOUR GOSPELS AND THE ACTS OF APOSTLES (MATTHEW, MARK, LUKE)


Birth of Jesus     (Matthew. 1:18-25)

i. Mary gets betrothed to Joseph by legal and traditional rites.

ii. Mary mysteriously conceives with a Holy Ghost-effected pregnancy

iii. Joseph resolves to divorce Mary.

iv. Gabriel warns him in a dream against that.

v. Mary gets delivered of a male child and names his "Jesus" as instructed by Gabriel.

Jesus' Baptism

Matthew, Mark and Luke recorded this while St. John only briefed through it, stating Jesus' visit to John during his ministry of baptism.

i. John proclaimed baptism for repentance of sins

ii. Jesus came to river Jordan where he was baptizing.

iii. According to Matthew, Jesus offered to be baptized by John at first refused, stating(according to John) that he was not fit.

iv. Jesus persisted, stating it was for the law to be fulfilled.

v. John baptized him and he came out of the water, then the following events ensued.

a. The heavens were opened

b. And he saw the spirit of God descending like a dove, a lightning upon him.

c. And a voice came from heaven, saying, "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased".

Significance of Jesus' Baptism and the Voice from Heaven

i. It commemorated baptism by immersion, significant of a true Christian's death and resurrection with Jesus.

ii. It was typical of a true Christian Jew

iii. Fulfilment of all righteousness

iv. It proved John's baptism and divinely approved it

v. It was a public approval of his Messiah ship

vi. It revealed God's approval of his Son ship

vii. It led to the Holy Spirit resting on him.


Significance of John's Baptism

i. It fully justified each repentant sinner.

ii. It constituted a new Jewish religious era.

iii. It was a link between law and grace.

iv. It was a shadow of Christ's baptism with fire and the Holy Ghost

v. It ushered Jesus into full ministry with an evidence of approval and power.


Temptation

Temptation means the process whereby a person is persuaded or lured to do something wrong using something pleasant.

After fasting for forty days and forty nights, Jesus was directed into the wilderness for Satan to tempt him. A explicitly recorded by Mathew and Luke;

i. First Temptation Satan tempted Jesus to command (a) stone(s) to become bread but Jesus replied with Deuteronomy. 8:3b "Man shall not live by bread alone…" (Matt/Luke 4:4).

This was an effort by Satan to make Jesus an economic messiah by using divine process to satisfy human need.

ii. Second Temptation (Third in Luke) - Satan tempted Jesus to jump down from the pinnacle of a temple, using Psalms. 91:11-12 that God's angels would save him. Jesus immediately countered Satan with Deut. 6:16, "you shall not tempt the Lord your God".

This was directed to making Jesus a magician and performing unnecessary signs of wonders

iii. Third temptation (Second in Luke) - Satan led Jesus to a high mountain and showed him thebeauty and glory of the cities of the world claiming he would give them to Jesus, only if Jesus bowed down and worshipped him. Jesus used Deuteronomy 6:13 to counter Satan; that only God should be served.

iv. This temptation was aimed at making Jesus a political messiah, ruling over earthly kingdoms instead of heaven.


Significance of Jesus' Temptation

i. Jesus primary aim for coming to the world was and is to bring to God and His kingdom(which is spiritual) and at for fleshy, false or political reasons.

ii. The word of God was proven very powerful that we can use it to defeat the enemy and should be memorized and used rightly.

iii. He revealed that eating bread was essential to physical living but obedience to the word ofGod leads to eternal life.

iv. Satan could also quote the Bible but always in wrong contexts and to achieve a desired evil means.

v. It marked the inception of Jesus' victory over Satan. vi. Jesus is not a magician.


Lessons

i. We should study, memorize and use God's word to overcome Satan.

ii. We shouldn't display our God-given spiritual abilities to impress people or to show them off.

iii. Since Jesus was tempted, every Christian would often be too, since he overcame, we too canalways overcome.


Call to Discipleship and Cost

Matthew. 4:18-25; 9:9-13; Markk. 1:16-20; 2:13-17; Luke.5:1-11; 27-32

Andrew and Peter, James and John; sons of Zebedee were two pairs of brothers who were first by Jesus to discipleship. They were fishermen who later became "men fishers". The other disciples were Philip, Bartholomew, Thomas, Matthew (Levi), James son of Alphaeus, Thaddeus Labbaeus, Simon the Canaanite and Judas Iscariot (or of Kerioth).

They were called first into discipleship to be mentored by Jesus and later into the apostolate. They spearheaded the Christian faith and disseminated the gospel during and after Jesus' presence.

Cost of Discipleship (Matthew. 8:18-22; Luke 9:57-63; Mark 14:25-33)

They include;

i. Losing/ forgiving material possession to following Christ.

ii. Taking up/bearing one's cross and denying oneself.

iii. Giving priority to godliness above ceremonial rites (Matthew. 8:22)

iv. Having no stable abode (Matthew. 8:20; Luke 9:58)

v. Give no room to comprise on assumption of duty. (Luke 9:62)

Lessons

i. Discipleship takes full determination and self-denial.

ii. Discipleship is rendering duty to God and man

iii. When a disciple, one should look up (i.e. compromise or backslide)

iv. Though it may be a little demanding, it gladdens the heart and increases knowledge.

v. It assures one of walking in the right

vi. Discipleship results in mastery and mentorship, since a master is one who was once disciple.

2. MIRACLES


A miracle is a supernatural event i.e. one that transcends the conventional dictates of nature.

Miracles were often worked by Jesus to reveal some mysteries of the Kingdom of God, heal the infirmed and physically challenged, deliver the oppressed, set the demon-possessed at liberty, raise the dead and to prove his omnipotence.

Types of Miracle

a. Nature Miracle: These are miracles worked immediately on natural thing e.g. water, storm, food

Some of such miracles include

i. Feeding of the Five thousand (Matthew 14:13-24; Mark. 6:30-34; Luke 9:10-17): Here, we see case a case of five thousand men (excluding women and children) being fed with five loaves of bread and two fishes which Jesus multiplied. The remnant filled twelve baskets.


ii. Feeding of the Four thousand (Matthew 15:32-39; Mark 8:1-10) As recorded by Matthew and Mark only, Jesus multiplied seven loaves of bread and few little fishes with which he fed four thousand men (apart from women and children). He did this out of compassion because they had been with him in the wilderness hearing the word of God for three days, lest they should faint on their way back home.


iii. Walking on the sea: (Matthew 14:22-26; Mark. 6:45-52).

On their way back to Bethsaida, the disciples of Jesus began to panic due to the tempest which befell their ship at sea. At about the fourth hour, Jesus showed up in the scene of fright and His presence caused more fright to the disciples. Jesus calmed them saying "Be of good cheer! It is I, do not be afraid."


b. Healing Miracles: These are miracles in which the infirmed, sick, and physically challenged are brought to good health. Some of such miracles are:

i.     Healing of the leper (Matthew 8:1-4; Mark. 1:40-45; Luke 5:12-16): Here Jesus had compassion on a pleading leper and healed him. Jesus charged him to tell no one but got to the priest ad show himself unto him and offer the gift that Moses commanded as evidence to them.


ii. Healing of the Paralytic (Matthew. 9:1-8; Mark. 2:1-12; Luke 5): Jesus acknowledged the faith of the four friends of a paralytic who let him down through roof because of the multitude. Jesus proved His ability to forgive sins and heal by forgiving the paralytic his sins and commanding him to take up his bed and go. The man obeyed Jesus' command and the people gave glory to God.


iii. The Centurion's Servant (Matthew. 8:5-13, Luke. 7:1-10): Jesus declaredthe faith climax He had ever seen, not even in Israel when a centurion whose servant was ill to the point of death came to Jesus. He told Jesus the sorrowful state of the sick servant and Jesus promised to come and heal him. This centurion implored Jesus to only speak and his servant would be healed declaring his unworthiness to receive Jesus under his roof.Jesus spoke the word of healing according to the Centurion's faith and his servant was healed immediately.


c. Exorcism: This is the process of casting out demons from their human bearers.

Some of such miracles are;

i. Healing of the Demoniac (s) (St. Mathew recorded two but St. Mark recorded one). (Matthew 8:28-34; Luke 8:26-40 Mark. 5:1-20): On getting to Gadarenes, Jesus saw the demon possessed, whose demons acknowledged his presence. They pleaded with Jesus not to be tortured. Though they had been torturing their victims, making them deserted to the tombs and mountains where they cried and cut themselves with sharp stones all the day. Many times the demon-possessed had been bound but kept breaking the chains and fetters, Jesus questioned their identity and they said "we are a Legion" i.e. very many.

Jesus then sent the demons into the swine as they had pleaded and the swine ran into the sea and there they died.

ii. Healing of a man with deaf and dumb spirit (Matthew. 12:22; Mark. 9:18-29; Luke11:14)

A deaf and dumb spirit had possessed a man from his childhood. It often cast him into fire and water and makes him foam in the mouth. This man was brought to Jesus and he cast out the demon and the people were marvelled.


d. Miracle of Resuscitation: This is one in which the dead is raised e.g.

i. Raising of Lazarus (John 11:1-44): Four days after his death, Lazarus is raised by

Jesus and the people glorified God.


ii. Raising of the widow of Nain's Son (Luke 7:11-16): A widow's only son had died and funeral rites were conducted. Then showed-up the problem solver, the great physician, Master Jesus and raised him, then a funeral became celebration. The people marvelled greatly and God was glorified.


Lessons

i. No matter the situation of our health, God has the power to heal us.

ii. The dead can live again by God's power.

iii. Jesus wen to about doing good, he was a good doer, we too should be.

iv. Jesus also gave his disciples power to work miracles, we too can do more that Jesus did if we believe according to Jesus (John 14:12)


Parable

A parable is an earthly story which has a heavenly interpretation.

Why Jesus Used Parables

i. To aid better understanding of his teaching using the physical to explain the abstract.

ii. To make the people more interested in his speeches, speaking only of spiritual things wouldn't be lively to them.

iii. To make the people morally and spiritually.

iv.To speak to his disciples alone who had understanding, leaving others (especially the scribes and Pharisees) in obscurity


Parables Illustrating the Kingdom of God

1. The Sower (Matthew. 13: 1-23: Mark. 4:1-20): The story signifies Jesus as a Preacher/evangelist. The story includes the sower, the word of God, the seed and the various lands, the hearts of men.The lands (hearts) and interpretation goes thus;

i. The seeds that fell by the way side and were eaten by birds: this means when a person hears the word of God and does not understand it and takes away what he has learnt.

ii. The seeds that fell on rocky or stony grounds. It means he that hears the word and is happy. But as the seed couldn't grow due to the rock's lack of moisture, neither could they develop roots, the word also has no roots in them and dies in such people's hearts because of tribulation, persecution or trials,

iii.The seeds that fell among thorns and were choked by the thorns. This refers to people who hear the word of God and materialism and avarice kills it, making it unfruitful.


iv. The seeds that fell into the god ground and became fruitful some in hundredfold, some sixtyfold, some thirtyfold: this refers to those who receive the word and understand it and it bears fruits in different quantities.


The Mustard Seed(Matthew. 13:31-32; Mark. 4:30-32; Luke 13:18-19)


This means the beginning of Christian faith, little at the beginning but gradually grows into significance.


The Wheat and Tares(Matthew 13:24-30; 36-43)

The wheat signifies the saved (believers or Christian) while the tares/weeds refer to lost/unsaved living together in the world, and waiting for the day of judgement. During this period of growth, the lost still have the grace to repent and be saved.

The harvest when the wheat will be separated from weeds in judgement when God will welcome His children into His Kingdom and others will be bound i.e. gathered together and poured into hell fire.



The Seed Growing Secretly (Mark. 4:26-29)

This parable illustrates he growth of the word of the Kingdom of God which occurs unawares in the hearts in which they inhabit.

Lesson

Jesus Christ has come into the world, paved the gapped bridge between men and God, revealing the mysteries of the Kingdom of God to men.

His disciples are the sowers, sowing the seed of the word of God into the hearts of men. Unbelievers should give the seed a good ground in their hearts, and believers should hold fast unto the truth and grow in faith and grace.


Parables Illustrating God's Love for Man

These parables reveal God's willingness to accept and restore any sinner who is repentant; His hands are wide open to them. All they need to do is to come to him soberly, humbly and repentantly. Such parables are:

i. The Lost Sheep:     Matthew. 18:12-14; Luke 154-7

ii. The Lost Coin:     Luke 15:8-10

iii. The Prodigal Son: Luke. 15:12-32


Parables Illustrating Man's Love for His Neighbour

TheGood Samaritan (Luke 10:30-37)

This is a story of a man who was robbed, wounded and censored by some robbers. A priest passing by saw him but took another route likewise a Levite. But a Samaritan (a Gentile a least religious of the three) saw him and helped him by taking him to the hospital and paying his bill.

Lessons

i.Samaritans were hated by the Jews because they were considered sinners. However, the so- called loved his neighbour and helped him. People should not be looked down on.

ii. We should be compassionate to mankind, just like the Good Samaritan.


Lazarus and the Rich Man (Luke 16:19-31)

This story is about Lazarus, a poor man and a merciless rich man. He never paid Lazarus any attention though Lazarus was daily laid at his gate.

After their death, Lazarus began to enjoy in Abraham's bosom while the merciless rich man was cast into hell.


Lesson

i.We should show compassion to the less privileged and help them.

ii. If we are ungenerous and merciless with our provision, we will be duly punished

iii. Wealth without salvation is not profitable as poverty with salvation leads to eternal life. (However, it is better to be wealthy and be saved)

3. SERMONS


Sermon on the Mount(Matthew. 5; 6;1-34; Luke 6:17-26)

Jesus rendered expressly to his disciples the joys or blessings of salvation and their demands. This sermon was rudimentary remark of spiritual and moral conducts and their benefits.


Characteristics of the Sermon

a. The Beatitudes

i. Blessed are the poor in sprit; for theirs is the kingdom of heaven (Matthew 5:1, Luke6:26)

ii. Blessed are they that mourn; for they shall be comforted (Matthew 5:4, Luke 6:21)

iii. Blessed are the meek; for they shall inherit the earth.

iv. Blessed are they which do hunger and thirst after righteousness; for they shall be filled.

v. Blessed are the merciful; for they shall receive mercy

vi. Blessed are the pure in heart; for they shall see God

vii. Blessed are the peacemakers; for they shall be called the children of God

viii. Blessed are they which are persecuted for righteousness sake; for theirs is the kingdom of heaven


b. Christians as the light and sale of the earth (Matthew 5:13-16)

c. Jesus's coming to fulfil, not destroy the law (Matthew 5:17-18)

d. Obedience to the word of God and righteousness enjoined (Matthew 5:19-20)

e. Reconciliation with one's brother and enemy is spiritually satisfactory (Matthew5:22- 26)

f. Adultery (either physically or in the mind through lust) decried imploration to forgo anything for the kingdom of God. (Matthew 5:27 - 32)

g. Precision enjoyed, swearing forbade (Matthew 5:33-37)

h.Endurance, perseverance and temperance implored during persecution (Matthew 5:38-41)

i. Freewill giving, enemies loving and God-like perfection preached (Matthew 5:42-48; Luke6:35)

j. Necessity of giving, fasting and prayer secretly. (Matthew 6:1-6; 6:18)

k.Necessity of prayer brevity (Matthew 6:7-8)

l. The Lord's Prayer: Component

i. Reference of God's name (Matthew 6:9)

ii. Fulfilment of God's will (Matthew 6:10)

iii. Prayer of supplication: Food (Matthew 6:11)

iv. Prayer for forgiveness (Matthew 6:12)

v. Prayer for Protection (Matthew 6:13)

vi. Wealth should be stored up in earth rather than on earth. (Matthew 6:19-21)

m. Serving God should be preferred to serves mammon (god of wealth) (Matthew 6:24)

n. There should be no anxiety for material needs because God will provide. (Matthew 6:25-34)

o. No man should judge his fellow man lest he too be judged (Matthew 7:1-2, Luke 6:37)

p. Hypocrisy decried (Matthew 7:3-5; Luke 6:42)

q. Ask, and God will meet your needs (Matthew 7:7-11)

r. If you do not like it, do not do it to others. Beware of false prophets. (Matthew 7:15-29)


Lessons

As a Christian, you should;

i.Be a peacemaker

ii. Be humble, loving and insensitive to persecution

iii. Glorify God and make other glorify Him.

iv. Totally rely on and trust in God.

v. Bear good fruits

vi. Long for the Kingdom of God

vii. Be totally holy and perfect, just as God is

viii. Be pure in heart and deed, giving no room to sexual pollution, physically or mentally.

4. MISSION OF THE DISCIPLES


Mission of the Twelve (Matt. 10:1-15; Mark 6:7-13; Luke 9:1-16)

The 12 disciples signifying the twelve tribes of Israel were empowered by Jesus and given power to cast out demons, heal the sick and the infirmed. They were sent in pairs to the last sheep of the house of Israel. They were commanded to take no gold, silver, brass, (Mathew only) scrip, two coats, shoes nor staves. Mark mentions their permission to take a staff. Mark and Luke mention their prohibitions to take money.

Achievements

i. They preached and some believed they were the first gospel preachers

ii. They healed the sick and infirmed

iii. They cast out demons


Missions of the Seventy (Luke 10:1-24)

Another set of gospel preachers were sent by Jesus to preach the gospel, 70 in all. They were also sent out in pairs, but this time to the whole world. "The harvest (souls to be converted) is bountiful, but the labourers (preachers to convert them) are few". They were permitted to take nothing at all, make no unnecessary or exaggerated greetings, heal the sick and cast out demons.

Achievements

i. The disciples returned joyful, stating that demons were subjected to them, but Jesus told them rather be happy because their names were written in the Book of life.

ii. They preached and souls were converted.

Distinctions between the Missions of the Twelve and the Seventy

a. The twelve were sent to only the household of Israel (Jews) while the seventy sere sent to the world.

b. The seventy were instructed to ear whatever they were presented to twelve weren't

Lessons

i. Just as disciples were sent out to preach, gospel preaching has also been assigned by Jesus to every believer. (Mark 16:15)

ii. Every true believer should possess power to cast out demons and heal the sick of the infirmity.

iii. We shouldn't rejoice because we perform miracles but because our rewards are in heaven.

iv. As Christians in the world, we are sheep in the midst of wolves; we should possess divine wisdom and protection to survive.

The Great Physician (Matthew 16:13-20; Mark 8:27-30; 9:18-22)

On entering the coasts of Caesarea, Philips, Jesus questioned his disciple on what people called him. They answered that some called him John the Baptist, same Elijah and some Jeremiah.


On questioning his disciples, about his identity, Peter answered "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God". Jesus attributed his knowledge of this truth to divine revelation rather than to human wisdom. Jesus then gave Peter the keys of the Kingdom of God and promised to build the church on him, warning his disciples to tell no on this truth for that period.


Transfiguration of Jesus

Transfiguration is the process whereby a person or thing is beautified the more.

Jesus took Peter and Boanerges (sons of thunder) i.e. James and John up to a mountain. Before them His countenance into a glorious state, a voice came from heaven, approving of His sonship and commanding obedience to Him.

Moses and Elijah then appeared to Jesus.Before then, Peter suggested that they built there Tabernacles one for Moses, one for Elijah and one for Jesus out of fear. A bright cloud overshadowed disciples and they fell in their faces (bowed down). Jesus then charged the disciples to tell no man the vision.


Significance

i. Moses represented the law while Elijah represented the prophets

ii. Their appearance to Jesus signified that Jesus came to fulfil the law and prophecies about him.

iii. It signified God's approval and acceptance of the son ship of Jesus.

iv. It revolved as Jesus' preparation for his imminent death.


Lessons

i. When we walk closely with Jesus, he reveals and teaches us things not meant for others

ii. God prepares and enlightens us for whatever task He gives us.

iii. When our hearts are pure and we live a holy life, we will see God in His glory.


Triumphant Entry and Cleansing of the Temple (Matthew 21:1-12, Mark. 11:1-14; Luke. 19:29-48; 62:11)

In Bethphage, an entrance into Jerusalem. Jesus rode upon an ass and upon a colt into Jerusalem which was a fulfilment of Zechariah's prophecy in Zechariah. 9:9. The people of Jerusalem joyfully welcomed Jesus spreading their garment on the way and strewing tree branches in the way, crying "Hosanna" meaning "save" or "help now"

Significance

i. It signified that Jesus is the King of the Jews. However, this kingship is neither political nor economical but spiritual.

ii. The messiah had always been anticipated by the Jews due to prophecies; these prophecies were fulfilled, for example (Isaiah. 9:6; Zechariah. 9:9)

iii. It marked a definite turn around in the history of the Jewish religion.

iv. The kingdom of David was restored and the kingdom of God was restored.


Cleansing of the Temple (Matthew 21:12-16; Mark. 11:15-19; Luke. 19:45-48).

When He got in Jerusalem, Jesus went into the temple and sent traders out with all their good, saying "My house shall be called the house of prayer, but you have made it a den of thieves".

Significance

i. It was after this temple purification that the sick and infirmed were brought into the temple and healed by Jesus.

ii. Jesus' made of corrected incited the chief priests and the Sadducees making them conspire against him and effect his death; his divine purpose.


Lessons

i. Jesus is the true Messiah

ii. Jesus has no room for indiscipline and corruption and pollution especially in the hearts which is his temple.

iii. If we welcome Him hospitably, Jesus is ready to triumphantly ride into our lives and cleanse us from all sins.


The Last Supper(Matthew. 26:17-30; Mark. 14:10-26; Luke. 22:7-23, Jn. 13:2-38)

Jesus had His last supper with His disciples and he announced that one of the twelve would betray Him, the person (Judas Iscariot) who He called unclean. Jesus also washed the feet of His disciples (according to John).



Significance

i. It was Jesus's last earthly Passover with his disciple, others will be in Heaven

ii. The Lord's Supper was established with involves eating bread (His flesh) and drinking wine (His blood)

iii. The bread eaten was unleavened which signified that there is no pollution or sin in Christ.

iv. Jesus' betrayal, trails, and death and Judas betrayal of Jesus were fulfilments of some prophecies.

v. Jesus displayed a token of leadership, humility, and leadership strife among the disciples.


5. TRIAL AND CRUCIFIXION OF JESUS


The Trials of Jesus(Matthew 26: 36-75; Mark. 14:53-72; Luke 22:26-71)

a. Before the High Priest: Since the Jews were under Rome, Jesus was tried before Caiaphas the Jewish high priest and was transferred to Rome because the Jews could not pass death sentences before the Sanhedrin (council of the elders who represented the people and the chief priests who represented the priesthood, a court). He was charged with false offences since He was innocent. This false witnessing was done by two people, they charged him with;

i. Blasphemy (that He was the son of God and that He would destroy the temple and re-buildit within three days and calling himself King of the Jews)

ii. Rebellion (against Caesar)

iii. False teaching (in Judea, from Galilee to Jerusalem)

The high priest questioned Jesus about the truism of his sonship to God. He answered "I am" (Matthew and Mark) Luke records the answer as "You say I am, but you will see the son of man at the right hand of power and coming back with the clouds of Heaven". This remark was considered blasphemy, thus the Sanhedrin declared him guilty. Jesus was then mocked, spat on and smitten. After this Peter denied Jesus on three different occasions.


b. Before Pilate (Matthew. 27:11-33; Mark. 15:1-15; Luke 23:1-5; 13-25; John. 18:28-40; 19:1-16) Jesus was referred to Pilate, the Roman governor for the death sentence. But Pilate found no offence in Jesus. He had the mind to release Jesus but the people pressured him greatly, accepting the punishment for their sins of wrong accusation. After returning from Herod, the people had Barnabas a notable prisoner released rather than Jesus. Pilate then washed his hands with water as a custom of innocence and handed him over to them too be crucified.


c. Before Herod (Luke 23:6-12)

When Herod saw Jesus, he was glad because he had heard much about Him, he even hoped to see Jesus work miracles. Jesus neither worked miracles to entertain him nor answered any of his questions. Herod and his soldiers molested him Jesus was worn a gorgeous robe and sent back to Pilate.


Crucifixion and Death of Jesus

a. Crucifixion and Burial (Matthew 27:32-66; John. 19:1, Luke 23:26-56; Mark. 15:16-17)

After judgment, Jesus was hand to a band of soldiers who wore Jesus a scarlet robe and put a reed (a sceptre) in his right hand like a king. Then they began to mock him kneeling and saying "Hail, King of the Jews ".

A man of Cyrene (in Libya, North Africa) names Simon was compelled to bear his cross. Above the cross was an inscription which goes thus;

i. According to Matthew; "THIS IS JESUS, THE KNG OF THE JEWS". (Matthew 27:28)

ii. Mark puts it as, "THE KING OF THE JEWS"

iii. Luke as, "THIS IS THE KING OF THE JEWS" (Luke 23:39)

When the go to Golgotha, Jesus was crucified. He was crucified alongside two criminals. One was repentant and forgiven; the other was unrepentant and died unforgiven


Words from the Cross(Matthew 27:46; Mark. 15:34; 43; 46) Seven words on the cross

i. "Woman behold your son, son behold your mother". (Jesus was concerned about his mother's welfare, thus, put her to the care of John, one of his disciples) (John. 19:26-27)

ii. "Father forgives them, for they know what they do". (Prayer of forgiveness for His crucifiers) (Luke 23:24)

iii. "Verily, I say unto you, today you will be with me in Paradise" (Promise to the repentant sinner (thief) who was crucified with him). (Luke 23:43)

iv."I thirst" (this reveals his humanity) (John. 19:28)

v. "Eli, Eli Lama Sabachtani? (My God, My God, why have you forsaken me?) (It was because of the sins of the whole world he was bearing at that time that God turned His back to him). (Matthew 27:46)

vi. "It is finished" (Indicating accomplishment of divine assignment) (John. 19:36)

vii. "Father into your hands I commit my spirit" (Luke. 23:46) (Dying He entrusted his spirit withGod).


Significance of the Death of Jesus

i. Jesus' death marked the total end of the Old Testament and the beginning of a new one.

ii. His death and blood are the price for salvation. (Colossians 2:14-15)

iii. It marked the defeat of Satan

iv. Broke the middle wall of partition between God and Man, Jews and Gentiles, making them one.

v. His blood which is superior to the blood of goats and ram is obtained

vi. His death cancelled the power of sin.

vii. It cancelled the reign of death

viii. Jesus' death bailed everyone out. He died the death everyone should die.


Lessons

i. Jesus is the Lamb of God who was slaughtered for the salvation of all.

ii. The blood of Jesus is needful for remission of sins

iii. Jesus was obedient to God totally, even to the point of death, we to should be

iv. Jesus so much loved and still loves mankind that He gave his life for them to be saved.

v. Jesus died, so we won't die again (spiritually) but live eternally (in God's kingdom)


6. THE RESURRECTION, APPEARANCES AND ASCENSION OF JESUS


a. Resurrection (Matthew 28:1-8; Mark 16:1-8; Luke 24:1-11; Jn. 20:1-30)

After Jesus's death, Joseph of Arimathaea who was one of his disciples got His body and buried it in His new tomb which he had hewn out of the rocks. This tomb was sealed and guarded by a watch under the auspices of the Scribes and Pharisees. These were efforts to prevent Christ's body from being stolen away.

On the third day as Jesus promised, He rose up from the dead.

Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James and Salome visited the tomb on the third day. They wanted to anoint Him with sweet spices. They saw that the stone had been rolled away and there had been an earthquake. Then they saw an angel according to Matthew, according to Mark, a young man, Luke says two men. The women became afraid according to Luke and Matthew, Mark says surprised calmed not to dread. The women were told that Christ ad resurrected and was in Galilee. The women then informed the 11 disciples of this.


Significance of Jesus' Resurrection

i. It is the point of Christianity

ii. It assures that we too will resurrect after death.

iii. It assures that Christians serve a living God.

iv. It was a fulfilment of Christ's prophecy of himself.

v. It connotes the perfection and completion of our faith as Christians.

vi. It was an accomplishment of Christ's divine mission.

Lessons

8. ST. JOHN'S ACCOUNT OF THE MIRACLES OF JESUS

A miracle is a supernatural event which exceeds the normal laws nature.

Turning of Water into Wine(John. 2:1-11)

Jesus and his disciples were invited to a wedding ceremony. The wine was finished and Jesus' mother, Mary told it to Jesus. After His unwillingness, Jesus turned one hundred and sixty-two gallons of water into sweet wine. This wine was confirmed better than the first. This wedding took place in Cana of Galilee.


Significance

i. It was Jesus' first ever miracle

ii. His power our nature was demonstrated

iii. The Jews brought the old and finished wine which mean false and insufficient religion (Judaism) but Jesus brought the wine which was sufficient and of better quality (Christianity).

iv. Vessels refer to the lives of men; the old and finished wine means the untrue and insufficient way of life. When Jesus is also invited into such lives he fills the vessel of that life with a better and adequate spiritual life.


Healing of the Noble Man's Son (John. 5:43-44)

Jesus was rejected in Judea, his native country and he said "A prophet is not without honour, except in his county. "He therefore departed into Galilee, into Cana. There a noble came to meet Him, imploring Him to come and heal his son whose sickness had almost led to death. Jesus complained that he was a miracle seeker and except he saw a miracle he would not believe. Jesus assured him of his son's healing and he believed wholeheartedly. Later a servant brought to him news of his son's healing. When this man inquired of the time he raised that it was exactly the time Jesus told him that his son had been healed.



Lessons

i. We should not seek Jesus' face for only his miracles but for his person.

ii. We should to run to Jesus when (but not only when) our situations are so pathetic and he will answer us.

iii. When God makes a promise, we should believe wholeheartedly and not compromise, and then we will receive immediate answers.


Healing of the Impotent Man (John. 5:1-9)

At pool Bethsaida which was in Jerusalem gathered many invalids/impotent; the lame, blind and paralyzed. They were awaiting the stirring up of the water. This was done annually by an angel from the Lord and anyone who gets in first was healed.

Jesus was particular about a man who had been infirmed for about thirty-eight years. Jesus healed him by commanding him to arise and take up his bed.

The man obeyed, though it was a Sabbath day.


Lessons

i. No matter how long a problem may be God is able to set us free.

ii. Though our relations may forsake, God will abide with us

iii. God's time is the best.


Walking on the Sea(John. 6:16-21)

One evening, Jesus' disciples were sailing across a sea towards Capernaum. The wind became contrary, tossing the ship here and there. The disciples were troubled and just then, the problem solver, Master Jesus comes on the scene. They were afraid, when they saw Him coming towards them, walking on the sea. He calmed their nerves and assured them that "It is I". He was welcomed into the ship and miraculously, the ship reached shore.


Lessons

i.When problems which seem greater than us arise, we shouldn't panic because Jesus is with us, to save us.

ii. Jesus is always available to help only if we accept Him into our ships.

iii. Just as the disciples welcomed Jesus into their ship, we too should welcome Jesus into the ships of our lives, for only then will there be a solution.


Healing of the Man Born Blind(John. 9:1-12)

Passing by, Jesus and His disciples saw a man who was born blind. His disciples asked which sin caused blindness, the man or his parents. Jesus answered that none of them but that God should be glorified. However, God is not the cause of this infirmity (Matthew 12:22) but whatsoever caused it, God would be glorified when He is healed.

Jesus healed him by making a mixture of clay and His saliva and anointing His eyes with it. He then said He should go and bathe in the pool of Siloam (meaning 'sent'). He obeyed and came seeing afterwards. This miracle caused controversy among his neighbours as to whether or not it was him. He ended the confusion when he said "I am the man"


Lesson

i. No matter our circumstance, Jesus is able to favorably change our stories.

ii. When Jesus intervenes in our situation, our problems vanish and that provokes a controversy among observers.





Drought of Fish(John. 21)

Peter and the other disciples had gone fishing all night until morning and caught nothing until Jesus' presence. Jesus asked them to cast their net on the right side of the ship to catch fish. They obeyed and the catch was so great (One hundred and fifty fishes) that hands were needed to draw out the net.

Lessons

i. We must follow divine instruction to succeed.

ii. No matter how long we have tried or been trying, Jesus us able to give us our desires. iii. We should always obey God.

Fruits of Justification

i. Freedom from guilt, making one innocent.

ii. Forgiveness of sins; making one righteous.

iii. Saves one from eternal destruction

iv. It qualifies for God's blessing

v. It gives one boldness to approach God boldly

vi. It makes one God-approval

vii. It qualifies one for God's mercy

viii. It frees on from the yoke of sin, shame, the flesh, the world and Satan.

ix. It gives one hope of eternal life.

Law is a body of rules which a group of people are to strictly obey, with suitable punishment for disobedience. God gave his Law to His people through Moses on Mt. Sinai (Exodus 20)


Fruits/Purpose of Law

i. It enhances orderliness among people.

ii. It burdens one with the yoke of sin, having no ability obey it.

iii. It was brought because of sin.

iv. It breeds offence

v. It breeds condemnation


Grace

It means undeserved kindness and care which one enjoys. It was brought by Jesus from God theFather through his death for mankind.


Fruits/Purpose of Grace

i. It leads to righteousness and justification.

ii. It leads to eternal life.

iii. It breeds divine love.

iv. It frees from the bondage of sin and guilt.


Significance

i. Grace is an amendment of the Law

ii. Law comes as a result of sin, grace came as a result of love.

iii. Law keeps in bondage of sin and guilt, grace frees from it.

iv. Law came by Moses, grace came by Jesus.

v. The Law recognizes the blood of rams, sheep, goats, etc. grace recognizes the blood of Jesus.


New Life in Christ(Romans 6:1-4; 12-14; I Thessalonians. 4:1-8; Romans 12; Colossians 3:1-7; Galatians 2:19-21; II Corinthians 5:17)

New life in Christ means one is dead to sin, flesh and their manifestations, and begins another life inn Jesus. The old man (Satan and sin) must be put away and the new man (Jesus and righteousness) must be put on. When baptism by immersion is observed, dipping into the water means dying to with Christ and as lifting out means starting a new life with him. Thus, the flesh should no longer be in charge of the spirit, but the spirit in charge of the flesh because the flesh brings death but the spirit brings life and peace (Romans 8:6) and those in the flesh cannot please God (Romans 8:8).

The manifestations of the flesh are; adultery, fornication, impurity, licentiousness, idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, anger, selfishness, dissension, heresies, envy, drunkenness, carousing and such Apostle Paul says those who do such things shall not inherit the kingdom of God. The fruits of the Spirit are love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faith, gentleness, self-control against these, there is no law.

Those in Christ should be dead while Christ lives in them (Galatians 2:20). They should no longer possess fleshy mind, but the mind of Christ (Philippians 2:5). They should desert the flesh walk in the Spirit and be filled with all the fullness of God. (Ephesians 3:19). They must be born of God and not be sinful (I John. 3:9).

Generally, whoever is in Christ is a new creature, old things are gone, and all things are new (II Corinthians. 5:17)


Lessons

i. Christ provides a new life for everyone who has faith in Him through grace

ii. Faith in Christ transforms one's humanity into a new one, making us to be like him.

iii. Life in Christ is a life of glory and perfection.

iv. Once one is in Christ, flesh and carnality dies, making him life in the Holy Spirit.

v. The Blood of Jesus when applied, washes away all sins and stains, making him new
