1. PRICE SYSTEM

This is a system where prices serve as a means by which resources are distributed using relative prices in a free market economy.

Merits of Price System

1.     It is used to decide what, how and when to produce.

2.     It determines factor payment, i.e. what to pay for land, labour, capital and entrepreneur.

3.     It is used by the resource market to allocate resources to producers.

4.     Price system ensures freedom of choice among economics agents.

Demerits of Price System

1.     It brings resources misallocation.

2.     It develops inequality of income since if emphasized ability to pay.

3.     Price system cannot provide certain goods, e.g. defense, judicial services, i.e. public goods that are not profitable.

4.     It creates unemployment problem due to instability in the production.

5.     It encourages a monopoly situation.

4.DEMAND

This can be defined as the units of the commodity an economic agent is willing and able to buy at a given price, at a point in time. It is the ability to pay on the part of the economic agent that makes demand effective.

Law of Demand

This law states that the higher the price of commodity, the lower the quantity demanded of that some commodity and vice-versa where other factors affecting demand are held constant.

Factors affecting Demand

1.     Price

It's a major determinant of demand. The higher the price, the lower the quantity demanded.

2.     Income

The higher the consumer's income, the greater his ability to buy more goods.

3.     Taste or Preference

Taste/Preference of a consumer influences a given good, irrespective of the price. Hence, the higher the taste of a good, the more he purchases the good, vice versa.

4.     Price of other Commodities

The price of other goods has an indirect effect on the demand for a product. The influence determines if the goods are substitute on complementary (joint demand).

5.     Price Expectation

If people think that the future price will rise, they will purchase more goods now at the ongoing prices. But if they believe that it will fall they will purchase fewer goods.

Types of Demand

1.     Joint Demand

This is a demand for goods that are used together. An increase in price of one leads to increase in price of the other, e.g. Tea and Sugar

2.     Derived Demand

It is a demand for a good not for its direct consumption, but as inputs for the production of other goods/e.g. cassava for garri etc.

3.     Competitive Demand

It is the demand for goods that are close substitutes, e.g. blue band (butter) and margarine, a rise in price of one leads to an increase in demand of the other.

4.     Composite Demand

It is a demand for a commodity which can be used to satisfy more than one need. Gas for example can be used for domestic cooking, electricity, industrial use etc.

Changes in Quantity Demanded

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

This law states that as the consumer consume more and more of a given commodity, the TU increases but at a decreasing rate (since MU is falling for each additional unit) reaches a maximum where MU is zero and begins to fall when MU is negative.

Cardinal Utility Theory

This theory is based on four fundamental premises.

1.Utility is measurable in units called units.

2. Utility is additive. A consumer can derive the TU from consuming a given food by just adding the utility from each goods.

3. Utility is independent.

4.There is constant marginal utility of money.

Ordinal Utility Theory

This theory came on board due to the inability of the cardinalist to measure utility satisfactorily. To them, utility cannot be quantified in form of utilities, but can be derived based on the ranking of different goods say A and B that give the same level of satisfaction. Emphasis is placed on indifference curve; hence, it is called indifference curve approach.

Assumptions of the Ordinalist Theory

1.The consumer is rational.

2. There exist two goods.

3. Consumers' preference is transitive (preference).

4. He does not get a saturation point. Hence, he will prefer a larger bundle of goods to smaller bundle because they give the same level of utility.

Indifference Curve

It is a graph of the locus of various combinations of two goods (A and B) that gives the consumer the same level of satisfaction.

Properties ofthe Indifference Curve

1.An indifference curve is downward sloping from left to right, showing marginal rate of substitution of goods A and B.

2. There are infinite numbers of indifference curve.

3. The indifference curve is convex to the origin.

4. The higher the indifference curves from the origin, the higher the satisfaction.

5. Indifference curve never interests.
